Abstract 7

Young adults frequently report cognitive complaints often attributed to sleep loss alone. However, 8

subjective cognitive functioning is shaped by broader lifestyle and affective factors. Cross-sectional data 9

were analyzed from 530 young adults (mean age 22 .1 ± 2.3 years) to examine the independent, interactive, 10 

and cumulative associations of short sleep duration, low physical activity, and psychological distress with 11 

everyday cognitive failures. Cognitive failures were strongly associated with sleep duration, physical 12 

activity, sleep quality, and distress in univariate analyses. However, hierarchical regression revealed 13 

that psychological distress, poor sleep quality, and short sleep duration were the dominant independent 14 

correlates of cognitive failures, collectively explaining a substantial proportion of variance in Cognitive 15 

Failures Questionnaire scores ( R2 = 0 .585 , p < 0.001 ). In contrast, the apparent protective effect of 16 

physical activity was not observed after adjustment for sleep and distress ( p = 0 .976 ), and no significant 17 

sleep-by-physical activity interaction was observed. Further, cumulative risk modeling demonstrated a 18 

robust dose-dependent relationship, with cognitive failures increasing progressively as behavioral and 19 

psychological risk factors accumulated ( ptrend < 0.001 ). Individuals exposed to all three risk factors 20 

exhibited more than double the cognitive failure burden observed in individuals with no risk factors. These 21 

results indicate that the cognitive burden in young adults can best be described by a additive increase of 22 

behavioral and psychological risk factors as a function of the co-occurrence, rather than by the presence 23 

of compensatory effects of lifestyle risk factors. Interventions aimed at preserving cognitive function may 24 

therefore benefit from simultaneously targeting sleep health and psychological well-being rather than 25 

relying on physical activity alone to offset cognitive burden. 26 

Keywords: cognitive burden; sleep restriction; physical activity; psychological distress; young adults 27

# 1 Introduction 28 

Young adult populations, particularly college students [ 1 ], frequently experience subjective complaints of 29 

brain fog, memory lapses, and attentional deficits due to the combination of high academic pressure and 30 

irregular lifestyle patterns. These executive deficiencies are usually attributed by the classic biological 31 

paradigm to chronic sleep restriction, a hallmark of student life widely assumed to degrade prefrontal 32 

cortical function [ 3]. Yet, this linear, single-variable causal model is increasingly challenged by emerging 33 

epidemiological evidence. 34 

Today, short sleep duration, high sedentary behavior, and elevated psychological distress frequently co-35 

occur, posing a complex, multifaceted danger to everyday cognitive function. Controlling for lifestyle and 36 

affective factors in multivariable analyses often significantly weakens the independent relationship between 37 

sleep duration and cognitive decline. This suggests that emotional distress and physical inactivity may be 38 

misattributed to sleep-related cognitive failures [ 4 , 5 ]. They are generally minor but frequent lapses in 39 

everyday memory, perception, and motor function, and serve as an early behavioral manifestation of disrupted 40 

cognitive maintenance. These subjective complaints are closely linked to emotional mood and perceived 41 

allostatic stress as they represent an individual’s lived experiences [6]. 42 

Physical activity (PA) has historically been theorized to serve as a neuroprotective moderator against 43 

these disruptions, with proposed mechanisms including the upregulation of Brain-Derived Neurotrophic 44 

Factor (BDNF) and enhanced systemic vascular health [ 7 ]. Some experimental evidence also suggest that 45 

exercise may preserve vigilance via affective and metabolic regulation, buffering the cognitive deficits 46 

induced by homeostatic sleep pressure [ 8]. On the other hand, sedentary behavior has been associated with 47 

elevated symptoms of depression and anxiety, independent of the mere absence of exercise [ 9 ]. Similarly, 48 

exercise-induced improvements in cognitive performance have been widely suggested across experimental 49 

paradigms [ 10]. But nevertheless, it remains fundamentally unclear whether physical activity serves as a true 50 

physiological buffer against the cognitive deficits induced by short sleep (a multiplicative effect), or whether 51 

sleep deprivation, inactivity, and distress merely accumulate to erode cognitive capacity (an additive effect). 52 

This study aimed to formally quantify the independent, interactive, and cumulative role of sleep duration, 53 

physical activity, and psychological distress on daily cognitive failures. By employing rigorous hierarchical 54 

regression modeling, false discovery rate (FDR) corrections, and pre-specified sensitivity analyses, I hypothe-55

# 2 Methods 59 

2.1 Participants and Procedure 60 

Participants were recruited through online dissemination of a survey link via university mailing lists, student 61 

networks, and social media platforms. A total of 530 young adults completed the survey (mean age 22 .1 ± 2.362 

years; 55.3% female). The study primarily targeted college-going individuals to investigate lifestyle and 63 

psychological correlates of everyday cognitive functioning in a non-clinical population. Participation was 64 

voluntary and anonymous, and collected no personally identifiable information. Individuals reporting 65 

diagnosed neurological disorders or current use of sleep-modulating medications were excluded to minimize 66 

potential clinical confounds. All participants provided informed digital consent prior to participation, and 67 

study procedures were conducted in accordance with the Declaration of Helsinki. 68 

2.2 Measures 69 

2.2.1 Cognitive Failures Questionnaire (CFQ) 70 

Perceived Cognitive Difficulties (PCD) were assessed using a 9-item adapted version of the Cognitive Failures 71 

Questionnaire (CFQ) [ 11], specifically selected to capture attentional lapses and memory failures in daily 72 

functioning. Questions were drawn from the original 25-item CFQ and retained on the basis of factor 73 

loadings from prior psychometric work identifying a unidimensional attentional-failure factor [ 6, 11 ]. Internal 74 

reliability in the present sample was acceptable (Cronbach’s α = 0 .78 ). Responses were summed to generate 75 

a continuous cognitive-failure score, with higher values indicating a greater frequency of subjective cognitive 76 

failures. Given the established conceptual overlap between self-reported cognitive failures and affective state 77 

[ 6], CFQ scores were interpreted as an index of subjective cognitive concern and everyday functional lapses 78 

rather than objective cognitive impairment. 79

2.2.2 Sleep Metrics 80 

Self-reported habitual sleep duration was evaluated both as a continuous variable (hours) and dichotomized 81 

into short sleep ( < 7 h) versus adequate sleep ( ≥ 7 h), following consensus clinical guidelines [ 2 ]. Sleep 82 

quality was objectively scored utilizing the Pittsburgh Sleep Quality Index (PSQI), a validated self-report 83 

measure. Further, sensitivity analyses additionally modeled sleep duration as a three-level ordinal factor 84 

(< 6 h, 6–7 h, ≥ 7 h) to confirm the robustness of the dose-response trajectory. 85 

2.2.3 Physical Activity and Sedentary Behavior 86 

Physical activity was quantified using metabolic equivalent (MET)-minutes per week via the short form of 87 

the International Physical Activity Questionnaire (IPAQ-SF) [ 12 ]. Participants were categorized into active 88 

(≥ 600 MET-min/wk) and low PA/sedentary ( < 600 MET-min/wk) groups based on standardized thresholds. 89 

Daily sedentary hours were additionally recorded as an independent continuous metric for lifestyle burden. 90 

2.2.4 Psychological Distress 91 

Mental health status was evaluated using the 4-item Patient Health Questionnaire for Depression and Anxiety 92 

(PHQ-4) [ 13, 14]. A threshold score of ≥ 3 was used to categorize participants into a high-distress group 93 

for cumulative risk modeling, while the continuous score was used in regression frameworks to maximize 94 

statistical sensitivity to the affective signal. 95 

2.3 Statistical Analysis 96 

Analyses were conducted in Python 3.12.3 using the pandas, numpy, scipy, statsmodels, scikit-learn, mat-97 

plotlib, and seaborn libraries. Prior to regression modeling, residuals from the full model were examined for 98 

normality using the Shapiro–Wilk test (W = 0.997, p = 0 .610 ). A minor deviation in homoscedasticity was 99 

detected using the Breusch–Pagan test (LM = 16.00, p = 0 .042 ), but this was deemed statistically acceptable 100 

given the sample size ( N = 530 ). Multicollinearity was evaluated using variance inflation factors (VIF); 101 

all variables demonstrated acceptable independence (maximum VIF = 3.91 for the interaction term, with 102 

all main effects < 3.01 ). Bivariate correlations (Pearson’s r) were calculated to assess preliminary relation-103 

ships. To control for type I error accumulation, all secondary predictor p-values were adjusted using the 104 

Benjamini–Hochberg false discovery rate (FDR) [ 16]. Groupwise mean differences in CFQ were evaluated 105

# 3 Results 114 

3.1 Prevalence and Bivariate Associations 115 

The cohort exhibited a mean CFQ score of 7.83 ± 3.04 (range 0.0–15 .9). Behavioral risk factors were highly 116 

prevalent: 50.2% ( n = 266 ) reported short sleep, 35.8% ( n = 190 ) met criteria for low physical activity, and 117 

34.2% ( n = 181 ) exhibited high psychological distress. The mean total physical activity was 882 ± 553 118 

MET-min/wk, while the mean global PSQI score was 6.74 ± 2.68 , indicating widespread decline in sleep 119 

quality. 120 

Bivariate correlation matrices revealed robust zero-order associations between the variables of interest and 121 

CFQ scores (Figure 1b). Higher cognitive failures were significantly correlated with increased psychological 122 

distress ( r = 0 .406 , pFDR = 3 .21 × 10 −22 ), poorer global sleep quality via PSQI ( r = 0 .567 , pFDR =123 

1.80 × 10 −45 ), and greater daily sedentary hours ( r = 0 .122 , pFDR = 0 .006 ). Conversely, CFQ scores were 124 

strongly inversely related to continuous sleep hours ( r = −0.449 , pFDR = 2 .94 × 10 −27 ; Figure 1f) and total 125 

physical activity MET-minutes ( r = −0.467 , pFDR = 1 .69 × 10 −29 ). Age ( pFDR = 0 .297 ) and smartphone 126 

usage hours ( pFDR = 0 .110 ) were not significantly correlated with cognitive failures. 127

those with low distress ( 7.12 ± 0.16 ; t(528) = 7 .824 , p < 0.001 ; Figure 1c). Similarly, the short sleep cohort 131

3.3 Hierarchical Regression Modeling 141 

To disentangle these overlapping phenotypes and control for shared variance, a hierarchical regression 142 

was performed. The final multivariable model (Block 4) was highly robust, accounting for 58.5% of the 143 

total variance in CFQ scores ( R2 = 0 .585 , F (8 , 521) = 91 .66 , p = 2 .45 × 10 −94 ). Stepwise integration 144 

elucidated the underlying behavioral hierarchy. Demographic factors (Block 1) explained minimal variance 145 

(R2 = 0 .005 ). The integration of lifestyle factors (Block 2) improved the model fit ( ∆R2 = 0 .360 ,146 

Fchange = 74 .42 , p < 0.001 ). Crucially, the addition of psychological distress (Block 3) captured a 147 

massive proportion of unique variance ( ∆R2 = 0 .216 , Fchange = 269 .71 , p < 0.001 ). The inclusion of the 148 

Sleep × PA interaction term (Block 4) provided only a marginal, non-significant improvement ( ∆R2 = 0 .002 ,149 

Fchange = 3 .06 , p = 0 .080 ). In the final model (Figure 2c), short sleep ( B = 1 .744 , pFDR < 0.001 ,150 

partial η2 = 0 .062 ), global PSQI ( B = 0 .590 , pFDR < 0.001 ), and psychological distress ( B = 0 .931 ,151 

pFDR < 0.001 ) emerged as the dominant independent predictors. Distress solely accounted for over 34% of 152 

the variance in CFQ scores (partial η2 = 0 .344 ), establishing it as the primary driver of perceived cognitive 153 

failures. Strikingly, the main effect of physical activity - which appeared robust in unadjusted bivariate testing 154 

- was entirely nullified upon controlling for sleep quality and distress ( B = −0.008 , p = 0 .976 ). Furthermore, 155 

the interaction between short sleep and physical activity was not statistically significant following Bonferroni 156 

correction for multiple comparisons ( B = −0.629 , p = 0 .081 ). 157

3.4 Sensitivity and Cumulative Risk Analyses 158 

To validate the sleep architecture findings, sensitivity analyses were conducted using alternative variable 159 

constraints. Evaluating sleep as a continuous variable yielded consistent predictive results ( B = −0.880 , p =160 

2.10 × 10 −22 ). Reparameterizing sleep into a 3-level ordinal scale confirmed a strict dose-response trajectory: 161 

compared to individuals sleeping < 6 hours, those achieving 6–7 hours ( B = −1.181 , p = 3 .85 × 10 −6)162 

and ≥ 7 hours ( B = −2.150 , p = 1 .51 × 10 −16 ) exhibited significantly lower CFQ scores. Interestingly, 163 

applying a conservative reclassification to physical activity thresholds (+20%) yielded a nominally significant 164 

interaction ( B = −0.846 , p = 0 .014 ), but it did not survive the pre-specified multiple-comparison threshold 165 

and should therefore be interpreted cautiously, and thus it remains secondary to the main additive effects. 166 

Given the absence of a robust multiplicative interaction in the primary model, I evaluated the additive burden 167 

of the primary variables (Figure 2d). Cumulative risk modeling revealed a highly significant, perfectly 168 

monotonic linear trend (slope = 2.187 , r = 0 .578 , p = 1 .33 × 10 −48 ). Participants with zero risk factors 169 

established the baseline cognitive failure threshold ( 5.30 ± 0.25 ). The addition of each subsequent risk factor 170 

escalated the cognitive burden sequentially: one factor ( 7.39 ± 0.17 ), two factors ( 9.37 ± 0.17 ), culminating 171 

in severe cognitive lapses for individuals enduring all three risk parameters simultaneously ( 12 .75 ± 0.47 ). 172 

# 4 Discussion 173 

This work demonstrates that subjective cognitive failures in young adults are driven predominantly by 174 

an additive accumulation of lifestyle and psychological vulnerabilities, accounting for nearly 60% of the 175 

variance in CFQ scores. In a rigorous multivariable context, the cognitive impact of physical activity 176 

is largely subsumed by the pronounced consequences of disrupted sleep architecture and mental health 177 

status. Psychological distress alone uniquely explained over a third of the variance in cognitive failures 178 

(partial η2 = 0 .344 ). While the CFQ’s nature as a self-report measure inherently overlaps with affective 179 

states [ 6], this covariance highlights how deeply shared the neural substrates of emotional regulation and 180 

cognitive tracking truly are. This psychological tax operates alongside the non-negotiable demands of 181 

sleep-dependent neural restoration, captured by the robust main effects of short sleep ( B = 1 .744 ) and poor 182 

sleep quality ( B = 0 .590 ). These findings align with systemic models of allostatic load, where chronic 183 

hyperarousal saturates executive capacity, leaving fewer neural resources for routine monitoring and memory 184

physical activity does not statistically buffer the deficits caused by short sleep once distress and sleep quality 186

the risk is strictly additive, following a severe linear dose-response curve where cognitive errors escalate 189 

dramatically as independent behavioral stressors aggregate (Figure 2d).In a biological system like ourselves, 190 

this architecture is likely bidirectional and recursive-distress fragments sleep, which subsequently saps 191 

the motivation for physical activity, trapping the individual in a feedback loop that heightens cognitive 192 

failure rates. Consequently, optimizing daily cognitive function requires moving beyond single-variable 193 

interventions. Because these behavioral burdens accumulate linearly rather than interactively, clinical and 194 

lifestyle therapies must comprehensively target this triad, prioritizing sleep preservation and distress reduction. 195 

Future longitudinal investigations utilizing objective actigraphy and high-density EEG will be critical to fully 196 

untangle these recursive dynamics and map the long-term stability of these neural representations. 197
