1 Abstract 22 

Sleep deprivation is associated with impaired endurance performance and an increased risk 23 

of running-related injury. Previous research has identified alterations in running 24 

biomechanics following a single night of sleep deprivation under laboratory conditions. 25 

However, whether these biomechanical changes can be detected using wearable 26 

technology remains unknown. Twenty-one recreationally active runners completed 27 

submaximal treadmill running under both normal sleep and total sleep deprivation 28 

conditions in a randomized crossover design. Biomechanical features were extracted 29 

simultaneously using a full-body motion capture system and a trunk-mounted wearable 30 

sensor. Five machine learning classifiers were evaluated in two classification tasks: a 31 

within-subject task using paired recordings from the same individual, and a between-subject 32 

task performed without individual baseline data. Within-subject classification consistently 33 

exceeded chance level for both measurement systems, with best accuracies of 85% for the 34 

wearable sensor (Logistic Regression) and 83% for the motion capture system (Random 35 

Forest). These findings indicate that sleep deprivation produces a systematic and 36 

individually consistent biomechanical signature during running. In contrast, between-subject 37 

classification failed across nearly all models and systems, with accuracies remaining close 38 

to chance level (~50%), demonstrating that inter-individual variability obscures the sleep-39 

deprivation signal in the absence of personalized baseline data. Both systems converged 40 

on temporal organization, loading-related variables, and stride-to-stride variability as the 41 

most discriminative feature domains. Contrary to expectations, the laboratory motion 42 

capture system did not outperform the wearable sensor. Together, these findings 43 

demonstrate that individualized, baseline-referenced monitoring is essential for detecting 44 

sleep-deprivation-related changes in running gait, and suggest that a single trunk-mounted 45 

wearable sensor may provide a practical solution for real-world monitoring when paired 46 

recordings are available. 47

2 Introduction 48 

Recreational running is one of the most popular forms of physical activity worldwide, with 49 

millions of people participating regularly across all ages and fitness levels (Hulteen et al., 50 

2017). While running offers well-established health benefits (Lee et al., 2014), it carries a 51 

substantial injury burden. Previous research suggests that as many as 65% of runners 52 

experience at least one overuse injury each year, with common examples including 53 

patellofemoral pain syndrome, iliotibial band friction syndrome, and medial tibial stress 54 

syndrome(Dias Lopes et al., 2012; Messier et al., 2018). These injuries arise from the 55 

repetitive mechanical loading inherent to running, where cumulative stress applied to 56 

muscles, tendons, and joints may eventually exceed a structure's tolerance 57 

threshold(Bertelsen et al., 2017). Importantly, fatigue is a key modulator of this process: as 58 

fatigue develops, running biomechanics change in ways that can introduce atypical loading 59 

patterns, potentially pushing tissues beyond their limits (Zandbergen et al., 2023). Because 60 

these biomechanical alterations are often subtle, objective monitoring tools are essential for 61 

coaches and clinicians working with this population (Mason et al., 2022). 62 

When assessing exercise-induced fatigue, laboratory-based techniques have frequently 63 

been used; however, wearable sensors have more recently emerged as a practical tool for 64 

detecting such fatigue, defined as the acute, task-related decline in performance during or 65 

following a sustained effort (Marotta et al., 2022). Evidence from these studies shows that, 66 

in running, peak tibial and sacral acceleration increases with fatigue, suggesting greater 67 

impact loading as fatigue develops (Schütte et al., 2015; Reenalda et al., 2016, 2019). 68 

Changes in shock attenuation between body segments have also been reported, with 69 

several studies observing increased head-to-tibia and sacrum-to-tibia attenuation under 70 

fatigued conditions, indicating altered impact transmission strategies(Derrick et al., 2002; 71 

Encarnación-Martínez et al., 2023). Beyond impact-related variables, only a limited number 72 

of studies have reported fatigue-related changes in spatiotemporal gait parameters such as 73 

stride length, stride frequency, and contact time, though these findings are less consistent 74 

(Schütte et al., 2015; Reenalda et al., 2016; Meyer et al., 2021). Collectively, this body of 75 

work demonstrates that wearable accelerometers are sensitive to fatigue-induced 76 

biomechanical shifts during running. 77 

However, fatigue is a multifactorial phenomenon that can arise from a variety of 78 

physiological and psychological stressors. Alongside exercise-induced fatigue, which arises 79 

from the accumulation of physiological and neuromuscular stress during prolonged or 80 

intense exercise, insufficient sleep represents a clinically important and underexplored 81 

source of fatigue for running populations. Insufficient sleep has been associated with 82 

impaired athletic performance and injury-risk in athletes, making sleep health an important 83 

concern for coaches and clinicians working with this population (Charest and Grandner, 84 

2022). A recent meta-analysis of 31 studies showed that sleep deprivation has a moderate 85 

negative effect on endurance performance, with longer-duration exercise (>30 min) being 86 

more adversely affected (Lopes et al., 2023). In addition, a 26-week prospective cohort 87 

study of 339 runners found that poorer sleep quality was associated with a 36% higher risk 88 

of running-related injury (Goldberg et al., 2025). Despite these effects on running-related 89 

injury, studies examining the direct biomechanical consequences of fatigue induced by 90 

sleep deprivation remain scarce. 91

The biomechanical consequences of fatigue induced by sleep loss cannot be assumed 92 

equivalent to the consequences of exercise-induced fatigue as described above. Unlike the 93 

peripheral muscular fatigue caused by metabolite accumulation or energy substrate 94 

depletion, sleep deprivation is more likely to influence motor performance through central 95 

mechanisms, occurring proximal to the neuromuscular junction. Beyond its well-96 

documented effects on cognitive performance(Killgore, 2010), sleep loss has been shown to 97 

impair motor performance in a task-dependent manner (Fullagar et al., 2015; Knowles et al., 98 

2018; Vitale et al., 2019; Walsh et al., 2021; Craven et al., 2022). In general, effects are 99 

more pronounced for continuous, multi-joint movements than for discrete, single-joint tasks 100 

(Fullagar et al., 2015; Knowles et al., 2018; Vitale et al., 2019; Walsh et al., 2021; Craven et 101 

al., 2022). This distinction is mechanistically meaningful: complex, continuous movements 102 

such as maintaining balance or walking rely on higher-order neural control processes, 103 

including feedforward planning, sensorimotor feedback, and executive attention, all of which 104 

are sensitive to sleep loss (Woollacott and Shumway-Cook, 2002; Pruszynski et al., 2011; 105 

Stuhr et al., 2018; Umemura et al., 2022). Running, as a dynamic, rhythmically coordinated, 106 

multi-joint task, is therefore likely to be vulnerable to these centrally mediated disruptions. 107 

Supporting this, recent work using a marker-based 3D motion capture system and 108 

instrumented treadmill confirmed meaningful biomechanical changes following a single 109 

night of sleep deprivation (Seynaeve et al., 2026). However, such laboratory-grade 110 

equipment is rarely accessible outside of research settings. Hence, this raises the question 111 

whether these alterations could instead be detected using wearable technology, a more 112 

scalable and ecologically valid alternative. 113 

A frequent challenge in detecting these condition-induced biomechanical patterns is that 114 

inter-individual differences in gait patterns routinely exceed the magnitude of condition 115 

effects, causing group-level analyses to wash out the very signal of interest. As has been 116 

demonstrated in the exercise-induced fatigue literature, the average runner does not 117 

resemble any individual in the group, and population-level models frequently mask 118 

meaningful individual responses (Chalitsios et al., 2024). Machine learning has emerged as 119 

a promising framework for addressing this problem, precisely because it captures 120 

coordinated patterns across many biomechanical variables simultaneously rather than 121 

testing each in isolation. Early research established the feasibility of this approach for 122 

exercise-induced fatigue detection. For instance, Buckley et al. demonstrated that a single 123 

inertial measurement unit (IMU) placed at the lumbar spine could classify non-fatigued 124 

versus fatigued states using a single group-level model with an accuracy of 75% (Buckley et 125 

al., 2017). Additional work showed that this could be improved substantially through 126 

individualization. Subject-specific models consistently and significantly outperform group-127 

based equivalents in identifying biomechanical shifts (Buckley et al., 2017; Op De Beéck et 128 

al., 2018; Dimmick et al., 2023). This performance gap exists because the most 129 

discriminative gait variables are highly subject-dependent; for instance, the specific 130 

mechanical features that signal fatigue or environmental adaptation in one runner often hold 131 

little predictive power for another (Marotta et al., 2021). To reliably isolate these subtle 132 

shifts, successful models typically require normalizing feature values against an individual’s 133 

unique baseline rather than using absolute population standards (Op De Beéck et al., 134 

2018). This preprocessing step is essential to distinguish true condition-induced signals 135 

from a runner's inherent movement style. 136 

In conclusion, sleep deprivation represents a clinically relevant stressor for running 137 

populations, with established consequences for both performance and injury risk. Yet, 138

despite the availability of both wearable technology and machine learning frameworks 139 

capable of detecting fatigue-related gait alterations, no study has examined whether sleep-140 

deprivation-induced biomechanical changes during running are detectable using these 141 

tools. Therefore, the primary aim of this study was to determine whether sleep deprivation 142 

can be detected from running biomechanics using data from a single trunk-mounted inertial 143 

measurement unit (IMU). In addition, we will (i) compare the discriminative performance of a 144 

laboratory-based motion capture system (Vicon) and a wearable inertial sensor (Runeasi) in 145 

capturing sleep-deprivation-induced gait alterations, and (ii) evaluate the relative 146 

effectiveness of within-subject, paired classification and population-level classification 147 

approaches. Both modeling strategies were applied across motion capture systems, 148 

enabling a direct comparison between personalized models that leverage subject-specific 149 

baselines and generalized models that operate across individuals. We hypothesized that 150 

sleep deprivation would be detectable above chance level, with superior performance 151 

observed for within-subject classification compared to population-level models due to 152 

reduced inter-individual variability. Furthermore, although both motion capture systems 153 

were expected to demonstrate discriminative capacity, we anticipated that the laboratory-154 

based motion capture system would outperform wearable inertial sensor owing to its richer 155 

and higher-resolution biomechanical feature space. 156

3 Materials and Methods 159 

3.1 Participants 160 

A total of 22 healthy, recreationally active runners (18–35 years) were initially recruited. 161 

Eligible participants were required to have adequate baseline sleep quality and duration, 162 

regular running habits ( ≥20 km/week for the past 6 months), and no running-related injuries 163 

in the preceding 2 months. Individuals with suspected sleep disorders, daily alcohol 164 

consumption, caffeine intake >400 mg/day, use of psychoactive substances, active 165 

smoking, or any known metabolic, cardiovascular, respiratory, or orthopedic conditions were 166 

excluded. One participant was subsequently excluded from analysis due to missing data. 167 

Descriptive characteristics are presented in Table 1. This study was approved by the Ethics 168 

Committee of UZ Leuven (S67790) and conducted in accordance with the Declaration of 169 

Helsinki. All participants provided written informed consent prior to participation. 170

3.2 Study Design 173 

The full study design and experimental protocol are described in detail elsewhere 174 

(Seynaeve et al., 2026). Briefly, participants completed two experimental sessions in a 175 

randomized, counterbalanced crossover design: one following a night of normal sleep 176 

(control condition, CON) and one following a night of total sleep deprivation (SDEP), 177 

separated by a one-week washout period (Figure 1). In the CON condition, participants 178 

slept in the laboratory from 11:00 pm to 07:00 am under full polysomnographic monitoring. 179 

In the SDEP condition, participants remained awake throughout the night under continuous 180 

supervision. The running protocol commenced at 07:30 am following CON and at 06:30 am 181 

following SDEP, and consisted of three 8-minute intervals at 90% of LT1, LT1, and 90% of 182 

LT2, followed by a fourth interval at LT2 speed continued until volitional exhaustion. All 183 

running trials were performed on an instrumented treadmill (M-Gait, Motek, Amsterdam, the 184 

Netherlands). Running intensities were individually determined based on lactate thresholds 185 

established during a prior incremental treadmill test. 186

3.3 Instrumentation 196 

Two motion capture systems were used simultaneously to record running biomechanics 197 

during each session. Laboratory-based kinematics were recorded using a 13-camera 198 

infrared motion capture system (Vicon, Oxford Metrics, Oxford, UK) sampling at 250 Hz, 199 

with 28 reflective markers. Concurrently, trunk accelerometry was recorded using the 200 

Runeasi system, a commercially available wearable sensor (Movesense, Vantaa, Finland) 201 

with integrated cloud-based processing platform (Runeasi.ai, Tienen, Belgium), sampling at 202 

200 Hz. The sensor was mounted at the lower back between L5 and S2 using a firm elastic 203 

belt approximating the center of mass. Both systems recorded simultaneously throughout all 204 

running intervals, allowing direct comparison of their discriminative capacity under identical 205 

experimental conditions. 206 

3.4 Feature Extraction 207 

For the lab-based motion capture system, biomechanical features were extracted from the 208 

spatiotemporal, kinematic, kinetic and joint power variables described in detail elsewhere 209 

(Seynaeve et al., 2026). Variables were computed over strides collected between minutes 5 210 

and 7 of the first three intervals and minutes 1 to 3 of the final interval. Only features 211 

normalized to body weight were used in the analysis. A total of 83 features were included in 212 

the final dataset. 213 

For the wearable sensor, raw acceleration signals were automatically processed by the 214 

Runeasi platform into 21 running metrics computed in 5-second epochs. Epochs containing 215 

data corresponding to minutes 5 to 7 of the first three intervals and minutes 1 to 3 of the 216 

final interval were summarized by computing the mean, standard deviation, and left-right 217 

asymmetry of each metric, yielding 36 features per interval per participant. Left–right 218 

asymmetry was calculated as the absolute, non-directional percentage difference between 219 

limbs, ensuring that only the magnitude of inter-limb differences was retained. The extracted 220 

metrics included spatiotemporal characteristics (cadence, flight ratio, flight time, ground 221 

contact time), impact-related variables (impact magnitude, impact duration, peak loading 222

3.5 Classification Task 227 

Two complementary classification tasks were evaluated. The first was a within-subject task, 228 

in which paired data from CON and SDEP sessions of a single participant were presented 229 

to the classifier. Then, the classifier was required to determine which session corresponded 230 

to each condition. This formulation explicitly removes inter-subject variability and isolates 231 

the intra-individual gait change due to sleep deprivation. The second was a between-subject 232 

task in which raw session measurements were classified as CON or SDEP without pairing, 233 

requiring the classifier to generalize across the natural variation in running gait between 234 

individuals. The between-subject task is more challenging and more generally applicable in 235 

a real-world setting, where a reference recording under rested conditions may not be 236 

available. Both approaches were applied independently to features derived from the lab 237 

system and from the wearable sensor, yielding four classification pipelines in total. In all 238 

cases, a single group-level model was trained across participants rather than subject-239 

specific models. 240 

3.6 Classification Models 241 

Five supervised classification algorithms were evaluated: Logistic Regression, Decision 242 

Tree, Random Forest, k-Nearest Neighbours (k-NN), and Extreme Gradient Boosting 243 

(XGBoost). Each was subjected to systematic hyperparameter optimization using a nested 244 

cross-validation scheme, with the number of retained features treated as a tunable 245 

hyperparameter and optimized jointly with the model-specific parameters. Analyses were 246 

conducted separately for the two motion capture systems (lab and wearable) and for both 247 

classification tasks (within- and between-subject). All analyses were implemented in Python 248 

using scikit-learn , XGBoost , and statsmodels . A fixed random seed (42) was used 249 

throughout for reproducibility. 250 

3.7 Data Preparation 251 

Within-subject task. For each motion capture system, subject and running speed, 252 

biomechanical features from the CON and SDEP sessions were subtracted to produce two 253 

signed difference vectors: CON − SDEP and SDEP − CON. This way, each subject 254 

contributed two data points per speed condition. This formulation eliminates between-255 

subject baseline differences and constrains the problem to detecting the directional shift in 256 

gait attributable to sleep deprivation. 257 

Between-subject task. The same subjects and speed conditions were used, but the raw 258 

session measurements were retained without pairing. Each observation was labelled as 259 

CON or SDEP, and the classifier was required to generalize across subjects without access 260 

to a paired reference. 261 

For both tasks, features were organized into a feature matrix X, a binary outcome vector y, 262 

and a group vector indicating subject identity. Running speed was encoded as a categorical 263 

variable with four levels (1-4), and used as a covariate in the ANOVA-based feature 264 

selection step. 265

3.8 Processing Pipeline 266 

For each cross-validation fold, a three-stage pipeline was applied strictly within the training 267 

partition to prevent data leakage. First , all features were standardized to zero mean and unit 268 

variance using a standard scaler fitted on training data only. Second , an feature selector 269 

based on an analysis of variance (ANOVA) identified the features most strongly associated 270 

with condition type. Specifically, for each candidate feature, a two-way ANOVA (Type II) 271 

was fitted with condition and running speed as factors, including their interaction term. 272 

Features were ranked by the p-value of the condition main effect and the top k retained, 273 

where k was treated as a hyperparameter. Third , the selected classifier was trained on the 274 

standardized, reduced feature set. 275 

3.9 Nested Cross-Validation and Hyperparameter Optimization 276 

A nested cross-validation framework was used to provide an unbiased estimate of 277 

generalization performance. The outer loop used 10-fold group cross-validation 278 

(GroupKFold) stratified by subject identity, ensuring no subject appeared in both training 279 

and test partitions within a fold. For each outer training set, an inner GroupKFold grid 280 

search (up to five folds, capped by available subjects) exhaustively evaluated all 281 

hyperparameter combinations using accuracy as the criterion, and the best configuration 282 

was refitted on the full outer training set before evaluation on the held-out fold. Classification 283 

accuracy was recorded per outer fold and summarized as mean ± standard deviation across 284 

ten folds. A chance-level baseline of 50% applies to both tasks given the balanced binary 285 

design. 286 

Hyperparameter search spaces were as follows. Logistic Regression: regularization 287 

strength C ∈ {0.001, 0.01, 0.1, 1, 10, 100}, liblinear solver. Decision Tree: maximum depth ∈288 

{2, 3, 4, unlimited}, minimum samples to split ∈ {2, 4, 6}. Random Forest: number of trees ∈289 

{50, 100, 200}, maximum depth ∈ {2, 3, unlimited}. k-NN: neighbors ∈ {3, 5, 7}, weighting ∈290 

{uniform, distance}. XGBoost: boosting rounds ∈ {5, 10, 50, 100}, maximum depth ∈ {2, 3, 291 

unlimited}, learning rate ∈ {0.01, 0.1, 0.2}. For all models, the number of ANOVA-selected 292 

features k was searched over {5, 10, 20, all}. 293 

3.10 Feature Importance Analysis 294 

To identify which gait features drove classification, each model was refitted on the full 295 

dataset and feature importances were extracted: absolute logistic regression coefficients for 296 

Logistic Regression, and Gini-based impurity reduction for Decision Tree, Random Forest, 297 

and XGBoost. k-NN does not yield intrinsic feature importances and was excluded from this 298 

analysis. Feature importances were computed separately for each sensor system and 299 

classification task. 300

4 Results 303 

4.1 Within-subject classification 304 

For the within-subject task, we classified the direction of the within-individual gait difference 305 

(CON − SDEP vs SDEP − CON). We found consistent above-chance performance across 306 

all models and both motion capture systems (Figure 2, Table 2). This confirms that sleep 307 

deprivation produces a systematic and directionally consistent change in running gait within 308 

individuals that is detectable by machine learning classifiers. 309 

For the lab-based motion capture dataset, the ranking was markedly different. Random 310 

Forest led with a mean accuracy of 82.7% ± 23.0%, followed by XGBoost (77.1% ± 19.4%) 311 

and Logistic Regression (70.0% ± 13.9%). Decision Tree (67.5% ± 19.3%) performed at an 312 

intermediate level, while k-NN (54.2% ± 20.2%) fell considerably behind the other models. 313 

This pattern suggests that the richer, higher-dimensional motion capture feature space 314 

contains non-linear structure better exploited by ensemble tree methods, while distance-315 

based approaches struggle in this higher-dimensional setting. 316 

For the wearable sensor dataset, Logistic Regression achieved the highest mean accuracy 317 

(85.4% ± 19.3%), closely followed by k-NN (77.5% ± 24.2%) and Random Forest (76.9% ± 318 

18.1%). Decision Tree (68.3% ± 20.5%) and XGBoost (72.1% ± 21.4%) performed at an 319 

intermediate level. The strong performance of the linear model again suggests that the 320 

within-subject gait difference captured by the wearable sensor is relatively low-dimensional 321 

and linearly separable, though the competitive performance of k-NN indicates a degree of 322 

local structure in the feature space as well. 323 

Fold-level variability was substantial across both datasets. The best-performing model for 324 

the wearable dataset (Logistic Regression) showed a standard deviation of 19.3%, while the 325 

best-performing model for the lab-based dataset (Random Forest) showed a standard 326 

deviation of 23.0%, with some folds yielding perfect classification and others falling near or 327 

below chance. This indicates meaningful between-subject heterogeneity in the magnitude, 328 

though not the direction, of the gait response to sleep deprivation. 329 

4.2 Between-subject classification 330 

For the between-subject task, we predicted session type (CON or SDEP) from raw gait 331 

measurements without paired reference data. This task proved substantially more difficult, 332 

with all models performing close to or only modestly above the 50% chance level across 333 

both sensor systems (Figure 2, Table 2). This sharp decline relative to the within-subject 334 

task demonstrates that inter-subject variability in running gait dominates the sleep-335 

deprivation signal when individuals are treated independently. 336 

For the lab-based dataset, performance was constrained across all models. Logistic 337 

Regression performed marginally best (62.9% ± 16.2%), followed by k-NN (53.5% ± 12.6%), 338 

Random Forest (54.2% ± 21.0%), XGBoost (50.8% ± 14.1%), and Decision Tree (47.5% ± 339 

17.5%). 340 

For the wearable dataset, Logistic Regression again achieved the highest accuracy (65.4% 341 

± 15.3%), representing the only model with a meaningful margin above chance. XGBoost 342 

(58.8% ± 8.9%) showed a modest but more consistent improvement over chance, notable 343

5 Discussion 409 

This study examined whether total sleep deprivation produces detectable changes in 410 

running gait biomechanics. Analyses were performed using two complementary motion 411 

capture systems - a laboratory-based full-body system (Vicon) and a field-deployable trunk-412 

mounted wearable (Runeasi) - and two classification tasks that differed depending on the 413 

availability of an individual reference condition. These analyses resulted in three primary 414 

findings. First , within-subject classification was consistently above chance for both motion 415 

capture systems, confirming that sleep deprivation produces a systematic and individually 416 

consistent biomechanical signature in running gait. Second , between-subject classification 417 

failed across nearly all models and both motion capture systems, demonstrating that inter-418 

individual variability in running gait overwhelms the sleep-deprivation signal when no 419 

reference condition is available. Third , despite their fundamental differences in 420 

measurement scope, both systems converged on temporal, loading-related, and variability-421 

based features as the most discriminative signals of sleep deprivation. These findings 422 

partially support our hypotheses: sleep deprivation was detectable above chance for the 423 

within-subject task, and within-subject classification outperformed between-subject 424 

classification as anticipated. However, contrary to our expectation that the lab-based motion 425 

capture would outperform the wearable by virtue of its richer feature space, both systems 426 

achieved comparable within-subject classification accuracy, and neither system supported 427 

meaningful between-subject detection. 428 

5.1 Within-subject vs between-subject classification 429 

Within-subject classification accuracy exceeded chance level across all models and both 430 

measurement modalities. This provides evidence that total sleep deprivation alters running 431 

gait in a sufficiently systematic and repeatable manner within individuals to support machine 432 

learning classification. These findings extend recent laboratory-based work demonstrating 433 

meaningful biomechanical changes following a single night of sleep deprivation by showing 434 

that such alterations are sufficiently structured to permit condition discrimination at the 435 

individual level (Seynaeve et al., 2026). However, fold-level variability was substantial 436 

across both systems, with individual folds ranging from near-perfect classification to near-437 

chance performance. This likely reflects heterogeneity in the magnitude of the gait response 438 

to sleep deprivation across individuals, although variability in model estimation and fold 439 

composition may also have contributed. Such variability is nonetheless consistent with well-440 

documented inter-individual differences in vulnerability to sleep deprivation (Van Dongen et 441 

al., 2004) 442 

By contrast, between-subject classification failed to produce meaningful discrimination in 443 

either modality, with most models performing within a few percentage points of the 50% 444 

chance baseline. As documented extensively in the exercise-induced fatigue detection 445 

literature, inter-individual differences in gait patterns often exceed the magnitude of 446 

condition-induced effects (Chalitsios et al., 2024) . The present findings suggest that this 447 

challenge extends to the sleep deprivation context. More broadly, these results support the 448 

subject-specific, baseline-normalized approach advocated in the fatigue literature (Op De 449 

Beéck et al., 2018) , indicating that the absence of an individual reference measurement 450 

substantially limits the reliable detection of sleep-deprivation-related gait changes. In 451 

practical terms, a single gait recording obtained without knowledge of an individual’s rested 452 

running pattern appears insufficient to reliably infer sleep deprivation status. 453

5.2 Wearable sensors vs 3D motion capture 454 

Contrary to our hypothesis that 3D motion capture would outperform a wearable system, 455 

both systems achieved comparable within-subject classification accuracy, and neither 456 

supported meaningful between-subject detection. However, the optimal model family 457 

differed notably between the two systems. Using wearable sensor features, linear and 458 

distance-based models (Logistic Regression and k-NN) outperformed tree-based ensemble 459 

methods (Random Forest and XGBoost), whereas the reverse pattern was observed for the 460 

lab-based motion capture features. 461 

The interpretation of this dissociation warrants caution, as model performance alone cannot 462 

establish the underlying geometry of a feature space, and multiple explanations may 463 

contribute. One plausible contributor is the difference in feature dimensionality between the 464 

two candidate pools. Large-scale benchmarking across 243 datasets has shown that the 465 

number of features is significantly correlated with the performance advantage of Random 466 

Forest over Logistic Regression, with the advantage shifting from negative to positive as 467 

dimensionality increases, while sample size alone was not a significant predictor of this gap 468 

(Couronné et al., 2018) . The lab-based motion capture system drew from a substantially 469 

larger and more heterogeneous candidate pool of 83 features spanning multiple 470 

biomechanical domains, compared to 36 features for the wearable system. If the effective 471 

dimensionality and structural complexity of the retained feature sets differed accordingly, 472 

this could partly explain why ensemble methods outperformed linear models for the lab-473 

based system but not for the wearable system. 474 

A further, non-mutually-exclusive explanation concerns feature intercorrelation introduced 475 

by the selection procedure. Because ANOVA-based univariate feature selection ranks 476 

features by their individual association with the outcome without accounting for redundancy 477 

among predictors (Michalak and Kwasnicka, 2010; Walowe Mwadulo, 2016) , features selected 478 

from the larger, more heterogeneous lab-based pool may carry greater residual 479 

intercorrelation. Whether this differentially affected the two model families is difficult to 480 

establish without formal analysis of inter-feature correlations in the retained subsets, and 481 

this explanation should be treated as a plausible hypothesis rather than a definitive account 482 

of the observed performance differences. 483 

5.3 Feature importance 484 

Feature importances were computed on the full dataset for the within-subject task to identify 485 

which features contributed most strongly to discrimination between rested and sleep-486 

deprived conditions at the individual level. Despite substantial differences in feature 487 

representation between the two systems, comparison of importance patterns revealed 488 

convergence across three recurring themes. The most pronounced concerned temporal 489 

structure: cadence was the dominant wearable feature across models, while ground contact 490 

time emerged as the dominant laboratory-based feature. These variables represent closely 491 

related components of stride-cycle timing. Therefore, their co-emergence as highly 492 

discriminative features across independent motion capture systems suggests that 493 

alterations in the temporal organization of running were among the most consistently 494 

discriminative signals of sleep deprivation. 495 

A second recurring theme involved loading-related information. Impact-related wearable 496 

features, including cumulative impact and impact magnitude, and laboratory-derived kinetic 497

classification. While trunk accelerometry provides an indirect representation of impact 499

mechanics. Therefore, these variables reflect related but not equivalent aspects of impact 501 

behavior. Nevertheless, their parallel importance suggests that loading-related information 502 

contributed substantially to discrimination across measurement contexts. 503 

A third shared pattern was the consistent contribution of variability-related features 504 

alongside mean-level variables in both systems. This indicates that stride-to-stride variability 505 

in key gait characteristics contributed meaningfully to discrimination between rested and 506 

sleep-deprived running states. Importantly, however, importance rankings alone cannot 507 

establish the direction of these changes; variability-related features may contribute strongly 508 

to classification regardless of whether variability increased, decreased, or shifted in a more 509 

complex multivariate manner. 510 

The two systems diverged in ways that reflect their different measurement scope. Wearable 511 

importance was distributed more diffusely across trunk-derived variables including dynamic 512 

stability, smoothness, and multiple variability metrics, whereas laboratory-based importance 513 

concentrated on specific joint-level kinetic and kinematic variables, reflecting the system's 514 

capacity to resolve mechanical detail inaccessible to a single trunk-mounted sensor. This 515 

suggests the two modalities may provide different but partially overlapping perspectives on 516 

the gait response to sleep deprivation, with the wearable capturing global coordination and 517 

trunk movement organization and the laboratory system isolating joint-specific adaptations. 518 

The patterns described above are consistent with several hypothesized neuromuscular 519 

consequences of sleep deprivation, and find support in the descriptive data. First, impaired 520 

feedforward control may play a central role. Leg stiffness at ground contact depends in part 521 

on anticipatory muscle preactivation, governed by feedforward mechanisms that allow the 522 

nervous system to predict upcoming mechanical demands and adjust muscle activity 523 

accordingly (Müller et al., 2010, 2014; Khajooei et al., 2024) . Sleep deprivation impairs higher-524 

order sensorimotor and predictive control processes (Killgore, 2010; Umemura et al., 2021) ,525 

which may reduce the precision with which muscle activation is timed and scaled before 526 

ground contact. This may leave the leg inadequately stiffened at touchdown, potentially 527 

contributing to the longer ground contact times, reduced cadence, and lower impact-related 528 

variables observed across both systems. Second, the shift toward reduced loading may 529 

reflect a conservative protective strategy in which load reduction is prioritized over stiffness 530 

and propulsive efficiency when precise anticipatory control is compromised, a pattern 531 

previously proposed in the context of extreme endurance running (Vernillo et al., 2014; 532 

Degache et al., 2016) . Third, the prominence of hip and knee kinetics alongside reduced 533 

ankle joint power in the lab-based dataset suggests a possible distal-to-proximal 534 

redistribution of joint work. During running, plantarflexors operate at a disproportionately 535 

high proportion of their maximal force and contribute a larger share of positive work than 536 

other muscle groups (Dorn et al., 2012; Kulmala et al., 2016) , making them particularly 537 

susceptible to fatigue. A compensatory distal-to-proximal shift has been observed during 538 

prolonged running to exhaustion (Sanno et al., 2018; Willer et al., 2021) , and it is plausible that 539 

centrally mediated fatigue under sleep loss may produce a similar pattern, though this 540 

remains speculative in the absence of direct electromyographic evidence. Finally, stride-to-541 

stride inconsistency seemingly increased across the majority of kinematic and wearable-542 

derived variability metrics. This is consistent with evidence that sleep deprivation impairs 543

mechanisms are not mutually exclusive, and because importance measures are 549 

operationalized differently across algorithms and are sensitive to feature covariance and 550

recurring discriminative themes rather than definitive mechanistic conclusions. 552 

5.4 Limitations 553 

Several limitations of the present study should be acknowledged. The sample size of 21 554 

participants, while typical for laboratory-based gait studies and adequate for the within-555 

subject design, limits statistical power and may contribute to the fold-level variability 556 

observed in cross-validation (Vabalas et al., 2019) . Replication in larger samples is required 557 

to establish the robustness and generalizability of the findings. 558 

The stability of selected features and model performance across resampling folds or 559 

participant subsets was not explicitly quantified, and the feature importance patterns 560 

reported should therefore be interpreted with caution. Feature importances were computed 561 

by refitting each model on the full dataset rather than aggregating importance estimates 562 

across held-out folds. This provides a descriptive overview of which features contribute 563 

most to in-sample classification, but the resulting estimates are exploratory in nature and 564 

not derived from independent out-of-sample evaluation. Moreover, importance measures 565 

are inherently model-dependent, differing across algorithms through coefficients, impurity-566 

based measures, or gain metrics, and are sensitive to feature covariance, redundancy, and 567 

model structure (Nicodemus et al., 2010; Tolo şi and Lengauer, 2011) . As such, they should not 568 

be interpreted as direct indicators of physiological sensitivity. 569 

The experimental protocol involved total sleep deprivation, which represents an extreme 570 

and relatively uncommon condition in real-world athletic settings. More ecologically 571 

prevalent forms of sleep restriction, such as partial sleep loss over multiple nights, may 572 

produce subtler or qualitatively different gait adaptations. It therefore remains uncertain to 573 

what extent the present findings generalize to more chronic or realistic sleep loss scenarios. 574 

The wearable system (Runeasi) processes raw accelerometry through a proprietary 575 

algorithm, and the exact computational definitions of several derived metrics are not fully 576 

transparent. This limits mechanistic interpretability and reduces cross-platform 577 

reproducibility of specific features. 578 

Finally, all running trials were performed on a treadmill at fixed speeds. Although this 579 

constrains ecological validity, it was a deliberate methodological choice: treadmill running 580 

allowed for precise speed control, which is essential given that speed directly influences 581 

cadence and other spatiotemporal parameters. It therefore remains unclear whether the 582 

observed gait changes would manifest similarly during overground running or at self-583 

selected speeds, where pacing strategies may shift in response to fatigue and speed can no 584 

longer be held constant. 585

Taken together, these limitations highlight the need for cautious interpretation of the present 586 

findings and for future work to evaluate their robustness across larger samples, more 587

6 Conclusions 589 

This study demonstrates that total sleep deprivation induces a detectable and individually 590 

consistent biomechanical signature in running gait, identifiable by both a laboratory motion 591 

capture system and a field-deployable trunk-mounted wearable. Within-subject classification 592 

performed consistently above chance across both systems, whereas between-subject 593 

classification failed, confirming that individual baseline data are essential for reliable gait-594 

based fatigue detection. This conclusion mirrors findings from the exercise-induced fatigue 595 

literature and extends them to a sleep deprivation context. Contrary to our hypothesis, the 596 

lab-based motion capture system did not outperform the wearable device. Instead, both 597 

systems captured complementary rather than redundant aspects of the gait response. 598 

Together, these findings provide a biomechanical and methodological foundation for 599 

wearable-based fatigue monitoring in runners, and highlight longitudinal, individualized 600 

monitoring as a necessary approach for detecting sleep-deprivation effects in real-world 601 

running contexts. 602
