Abstract 20 

Mind Blanking (MB) is a mental state characterized by the experience of a seemingly empty mind .21 

This apparent emptiness challenges the theoretical view of a continuous and content -full stream of 22 

thought , leading to the question of how it is possible to capt ur e MB events in the first place . MB 23 

research has relied largely on probe -caught experience -sampling , during which participants are 24 

interrupted at random times to report their mental state. This approach risks missing some MB 25 

events a s they may occur between probes. Self -caught paradigms, on the other hand, allow 26 

participants to report MB upon spontaneously realizing it, relying on meta -awareness . To date, it 27 

is not clear whether these two methods capture the same phenomenon neuronally. Here, we 28 

investigated whether probe -caught MB (pMB) and self -caught MB (sMB) converge on the ir 29 

behavioral properties and brain correlates . Twenty -two participants underwent 3T fMRI scanning 30 

while performing both a pMB and a sMB experience -sampling task . We compared the behavioral 31 

properties , BOLD activations and time -varying functional connectivity (FC) across the two 32 

approaches . We found that reporting frequenc ies of pMB and sMB were significantly correlated 33 

across individuals , and both MB r eports were uniformly distributed over time, pointing towards a 34 

behavioral convergence across approaches. However, we observed a divergence in BOLD 35 

activations, as sMB recruited significantly greater activation in the dorsal anterior cingulate cortex 36 

(dACC) compared to pMB, likely reflecting the meta -awareness and metacognitive monitoring 37 

required for self -detecting a MB. Lastly , t ime -varying FC analysis revealed a convergence, with 38 

both pMB and sMB FC resembling a hyperconnec tivity pattern compared to mind wandering ,39 

pointing towards low arousal FC. Together, our results show that sMB and pMB converge on 40 

overlapping neurobehavioral correlates . This positions the self -caught method as a complementary 41 

tool for studying MB without external interruption , while highlighting the role of meta -awareness 42 

in self -report detection. 43

Introductio n45 

While wakefulness is typically characterized by a continuous stream of thought, individuals can 46 

experience periods of a seemingly empty mind. This experience is linked to a mental state known 47 

as Mind Blanking (MB) , formally defined as a waking state in which individuals report no mental 48 

content or are unable to describe their immediate experience (Andrillon et al., 2025; Boulakis & 49 

Demertzi, 2025; Ward & Wegner, 2013) . So far, research into MB has primarily relied on probe -50 

caught methodologies, where participants are interrupted to report their immediate mental state at 51 

random moments (e.g., “What was on your mind just now?”) . However, individuals can also 52 

spontaneously catch themselves in a blank state without an external prompt (i.e., self -caught) . To 53 

the best of our knowledge, only one study so far has used the self -caught paradigm to characterize 54 

MB behaviorally (Ward & Wegner, 2013) , while their brain correlates have not been explored. 55 

Consequently, it remains unknown whether these two reporting methodologies capture the same 56 

neurobiological phenomenon .57 

Probe -caught methods have been extensively used both during resting state (Boulakis et al., 2025; 58 

Koroma et al., 2023; Unsworth et al., 2021; Van Calster et al., 2017) and during cognitive tasks 59 

(Andrillon et al., 2021; Huijser et al., 2020; Robison et al., 2019, 2020; Unsworth et al., 2025) .60 

These studies have shown that probe -caught MB (pMB) events are reported sparsely (Boulakis et 61 

al., 2026) , but uniformly across probes (Mortaheb et al., 2022) . Converging evidence from 62 

neuroimaging studies points towards reduced cortical excitability and hyper -synchrony during 63 

pMB. First, the brain elicits parietal slow wave activity during pMB reports that, despite its 64 

localized nature, morphologically resembles the slow waves observed globally during deep sleep 65 

(Andrillon et al., 2021) . Second, the brain shows widespread deactivations across cortical areas 66 

(Boulakis et al., 2023) . Third, functional connectivity (FC) during pMB self -organizes in a 67 

hyperconnected pattern where all regions are synchronized together (Mortaheb et al., 2022) as also 68 

observed in unconscious states (Aedo -Jury et al., 2020; El -Baba et al., 2019) . However, this 69 

relationship seems to be mediated by perceived arousal and task context . For instance, during a 70 

sustained attentional task , pMB has been associated with the typical anticorrelated FC patte rn71 

observed in wakefulness across vigilance levels, yet was associated with the hyperconnected 72 

pattern only during self -reported high vigilance levels, potentially due to hyperconnectivity being 73 

more dominant across mental states during sleepiness (Boulakis, Kusztor, et al., 2025) . Lastly, 74

pMB reports increase in states of low arousal (Boulakis et al., 2025) and are characterized by 75 

higher fMRI global signal (GS) amplitude , a proxy of arousal level (Liu et al., 2017; Wong et al., 76 

2013) , compared to other mental states (Mortaheb et al., 2022) .77 

Even though probe -caught methods are valuable, they possess an inherent limitation: they rely on 78 

externally timed cues and may miss episodes that occur between probes. Indeed, MB in probe -79 

caught methodologies is typically captured by 10% of the probes (Boulakis et al., 2026) , rendering 80 

it a highly time -consuming methodology. A pproache s that do not depend on external sampling 81 

could therefore offer a more efficient alternative by capturing the entire sampling period and 82 

allowing for the unconstrained accumulation of reports . A promising alternative is the self -caught 83 

paradigm, which has been widely applied in mind wandering (MW) research (Chu et al., 2023) 84 

but rarely to MB (Ward & Wegner, 2013) . In this method, participants are instructed to report 85 

whenever they notice or ‘catch’ themselves in a specific mental state. By design, this approach 86 

captures MB events with meta -awareness (Chu et al., 2023) , meaning one must realize their mind 87 

feels empty in order t o report it (i.e., awareness of absence) (Mazor & Fleming, 2020) . Given that 88 

this introduces a distinct metacognitive demand , and because the paradigm has rarely been applied 89 

to MB, it remains unknown whether sMB shares the same behavioral properties and brain 90 

correlates as pMB or if the act of self -catching fundamentally alters its phenomenological and 91 

neurophysiological correlates. 92 

The present study aims to compare self -caught and probe -caught methodologies and to investigate 93 

whether sMB shares the established behavioral properties and neural correlates of pMB . While we 94 

expect the core signature of this state to be similar, we hypothesize that sMB recruits additional 95 

brain regions related to meta -awareness and metacognitive monitorin g.96

Desi gn & Tasks 106 

The study employed a within -subject design, in which participants performed two experience -107 

sampling tasks in a 3T MRI scanner (Magnetom Prisma , Siemens Medical Solutions, Erlangen, 108 

Germany) : one with the self -caught, and the other with the probe -caught methodology. Tasks were 109 

performed in two sessions separated by a 15 -minute break . The first session included the self -110 

caught task , alongside other cognitive task s outside the scope of th e present study . The second 111 

session comprised the probe -caught task and diffusion -weighted imagin g, which is also outside 112 

the scope of this study. In the self -caught task (approx. 8 min duration), participants were asked to 113 

press a button whenever they felt they had an MB event . In the probe -caught task (approx. 19 min 114 

duration), participants were interrupted at random times by auditory and visual probes (n=25, inter -115 

probe interval: 30 -60s), inviting them to use a button press to report their immediate mental state. 116 

Participants were asked to report if their thoughts were in the “past”, “future” , “present”, or simply 117 

“nowhere” ( MB ). When participants choose any of the first two options, they were presented with 118 

a second question asking if they were thinking of themselves or someone else (all coded as MW) .119 

When they selected “present” they were asked if their thought was stimulus dependent (Sensation s, 120 

Sens ) or independent (MW). This multi -step questioning tree was designed to mirror the specific 121 

dimensions of time, self -referentiality, and stimulus -dependence manipulated across the broader 122 

experimental protocol. However, because these granular distinctions are outside the scope of the 123 

present study, reports were collapsed into broader categories (MB, MW, and Sensations) for 124 

analysis. Frequency of other mental states aside from MB can be found in Supplementary 1. If a 125 

response took longer than 10 seconds, it was coded as an error and excluded from the analysis. 126 

The tasks were implemented in MATLAB using the Psychophysics Toolbox (PTB) (Brainard, 127 

1997) and are available at  https://gitlab.uliege.be/poc/self -caught -mb . Experimental design and 128 

analysis are illustrated in  Figure 1. During both tasks, participants were in a resting state , instructed 129 

to let their mind wander, with eyes open, fixating on a black screen. For each task, we defined rest 130 

and mental state periods. For the probe -caught task, m ental states pMB , MW, and Sens) were 131 

defined as the 10s before the probe  (Boulakis, Kusztor, et al., 2025; Mortaheb et al., 2022; Van 132

Analysis 168 

Behavioral data 169 

To assess the consistency of individual MB report s across conditions, we computed the Spearman 170 

correlation between the number of MB reports per subject for each task . Additionally, given our 171 

small sample size, we also computed the Kendall Rank co efficient for complementary robustness .172 

This analysis aimed to evaluate whether MB occurrence reflects a stable individual characteristic, 173 

whereby participants who report more MB in one task also tend to report more in the other. 174 

Following previous st udies (Mortaheb et al., 2022) we also examined the temporal distribution of 175 

MB reports within each task . Acquisition time was divided into 5 equal -duration bins, and the 176 

number of MB reports in each bin was counted. We then compared the resulting distribution 177 

against a uniform distribution using a χ² test, and calculated effect size using φ (φ = √(χ²/ n)). 178 

Additionally , to test for evidence in favor of the null hypothesis (uniformity) , we computed a Bayes 179 

factor comparing a uniform distribution (all bins equally likely) against a non -uniform distribution 180 

multinomial model with a symmetric Dirichlet(1) prior , where BF₁₀ < 0.33 indicates evidence for 181 

uniformity (null hypothesis) , whereas values between 0.33 and 1 were considered inconclusive .182

Global signal (GS) amplitude as a proxy for arousal fluctuations across mental states 244 

We considered GS as the average BOLD activity across whole -brain voxels (Fox et al., 2009) . As 245 

mentioned, GS amplitude serves as an indirect proxy for arousal levels , where higher amplitude 246 

typically indicates lower arousal and vice vers a (Liu et al., 2017; Wong et al., 2013) . Given that 247 

MB is often associated with low arousal, we investigated whether GS magnitude (i.e., the L2 norm 248 

of the global signal time course during each mental state) differed between mental state reports. 249

timepoints. We calculated the L2 norm for each event to obtain a single GS magnitude value per 255

GS magnitude was modeled using a Generalized Linear Mixed -Effects Model , including mental 257 

state reports (sMB, pMB, Sens, MW) as a fixed effect and a random intercept per subject. Given 258 

that GS magnitude values are strictly positive, we utilized a gamma distribution with a log link 259 

function. Post -hoc comparisons between mental states were performed using marginal means. All 260 

reported p-values were adjusted for multiple comparisons using the Tukey method. We 261 

additionally tested whether the global -signal effect could be explained by task duration by 262 

including total timepoints as a covariate (Supplementary 6). 263

We here compared two self -report methodologies for capturing MB events during typical waking 391 

time , the widely -used probe -caught method (Boulakis et al., 2025; Koroma et al., 2023; Munoz -392 

Musat et al., 2025; Robison et al., 2019; Unsworth et al., 2021; Van Calster et al., 2017) , and the 393 

self -caught method (Ward & Wegner, 2013) . Our results indicate that both methods successfully 394 

capture MB episodes, which share similar behavioral properties and are characterized by 395 

hyperconnectivity across the brain. Where these states diverged was in BOLD activation, with 396 

sMB recruiting greater dACC activation compared to pMB .397 

A primary contribution of this work is the exploration of sMB, demonstrating that sMB shares 398 

convergent neurobiological correlates with pMB . Unlike previous paradigms that required 399 

participants to intentionally “empty their minds” (Kawagoe et al., 2019) , the current approach 400 

allows participants to report if they notice their mind went blank spontaneously . The significant 401 

correlation between sMB and pMB frequencies across individuals suggests high cross -method 402 

consistency, indicating that both detection methods track individual differences in the propensity 403 

to experience MB within the experimental environment . Furthermore, the uniform temporal 404 

distribution of reports in both tasks aligns with previous evidence suggesting that MB occurrence 405 

is not time -dependent , but a default mental state takin g place in the stream of ongoing thinking 406 

(Mortaheb et al., 2022) . While Ward and Wegner (2013) also propose d that overall MB reports 407 

appear ed stead il y over time, they observed a diverging trend over time where pMB reports 408 

increased while sMB decreased . It should be noted, though, that those find ing s were observed 409 

during a task condition (reading) and over a longer duration (20 min) , which could have led to a 410 

lower capacity to monitor one’s stream of thought due to fatigue . In the present analysis, both tasks 411 

took place at a shorter resting -state context (8 min), which could have permitted the preservation 412 

of sufficient vigilance so that both detection methods led to capture the MB phenomenon stabl y413 

across time .414 

Additionally, we show that with the probe -caught task, MB was reported in approximately 10% of 415 

probes, which is consistent with previous literature at large (for a meta -analysis on MB rates see 416 

Boulakis et al., 2026) . However, given that the frequency of these reports is probe -dependent, 417 

episodes occurring between probes are inherently missed. While s elf -caught reports were more 418 

frequent in absolute count , the two methods differed in sampling rate and task duration, making a 419 

direct numerical comparison difficult : self -caught reports were collected continuously over 420

acquisition time, whereas probe -caught reports were constrained to the probes . Nevertheless, this 421 

descriptive pattern aligns with Ward and Wegner (2013) , who similarly observed that sMB events 422 

consistently outnumber ed pMB episodes during a reading task . Our results point in a similar 423 

direction and extend this trend to a resting -state condition , notwithstanding the task instruction to 424 

self -detect and report these blank moments. 425 

We consider that the higher absolute frequency of sMB relative to pMB suggests a critical 426 

methodological trade -off. While the self -caught method provides a continuous real -time reporting 427 

that can capture events that would otherwise be missed between intermittent p robes , the explicit 428 

requirement for metacognitive assessment of sMB may create a dual bias. On the one side , it might 429 

redirect the participant’s focus to internal monitoring, thereby increasing false positive detection 430 

of MB (i.e., over -sensitivity). On the other side, it could as well paradoxically underestimate true 431 

frequency , because the continuous cognitive load of self -monitoring may actively prevent the mind 432 

from entering a blank state. Moreover, as self -caught methodologies by definition only capture 433 

mental states with meta -awareness (Chu et al., 2023) , they necessarily exclude instances without 434 

meta -awareness. A possible alternative for future research to resolve this reporting paradox is to 435 

move toward s report -independent decoding techniques. For instance, by using the neural 436 

signatures (i.e., hyperconnectivity, decrease in GS) as features in a decoder scheme , it may be 437 

possible to validate the occurrence of MB episodes independent of the participant’s active report 438 

in a more representative way . Initial efforts to decode MB from brain markers (Munoz -Musat et 439 

al., 2025) , as well as from body -brain signals (Boulakis et al., 2025) , have already been proved 440 

successful. Indeed, these protocols involved training classification algorithms that relied on MB 441 

reports as the ground truth. While Boulakis et al. (2025) applied these models to predict other 442 

labeled MB reports, Munoz -Musat et al. (2025) took an initial step towards extrapolation by 443 

applying their trained classifier to unprobed, unlabeled data blocks during a cognitive task. By 444 

crossing these unlabeled predictions against behavioral performance (e.g., increased miss rates and 445 

prolonged reaction times), they provided an indirect behavioral validation of the decoded MB 446 

episodes. Ultimately, efforts to develop classification schemes free from reports could provide the 447 

ideal middle alter native: they do not confound MB with the detection itself (like self -caught 448 

methods) , but also allow for a continuous tracking without being limited to discrete sampling 449 

windows (like probe -caught methods). 450

Regarding the activation analysis, we consider that the finding that sMB recruits the dACC 451 

compared to pMB likely reflects aspects of meta -awareness, meta -cognitive evaluation, and 452 

monitoring processing required for self -detection (Mazor & Fleming, 2020) . Indeed, sMB requires 453 

active, continuous meta -awareness to detect a MB event where one must not only be in a MB state, 454 

but also become aware of being in that state, while pMB relies on a passive, reactive appraisal 455 

prompted by the external probe (Chu et al., 2023) . As the ACC is tightly involved in metacognition 456 

(Vaccaro & Fleming, 2018) and self -referential processing (D’Argembeau et al., 2007) , we propose 457 

this activation reflects the internal evaluation of their ongoing cognition. Activity on the ACC has 458 

only been observed during MB when participants are asked to empty their minds intentionally 459 

(Kawagoe et al., 2019) , but no such activity has been observed during spontaneous pMB (Boulakis 460 

et al., 2023) . This is also reflected in the time -varying functional connectivity pattern, as sMB was 461 

associated with a pattern of high within -somatosensory network connectivity, which was not the 462 

case for pMB. This could be tapping into distinct nuances of MB: with and without meta -463 

awareness, as similarly observed for MW  (Christoff et al., 2009; Chu et al., 2023; Seli et al., 2017) .464 

Aside from BOLD divergences, both MB capturing methodologies converged on their connectome 465 

signature. We found that both MB events were only consistently associated with the 466 

hyperconnected brain state (Pattern 4) , which lacks the variability in connectivity linked to typical 467 

content -full cognition (Deco et al., 2017) . It is important to mention that t his hyperconnected brain 468 

pattern has also been observed in states o f reduced consciousness , such as during sleep and under 469 

anesthesia (Aedo -Jury et al., 2020) , which reinforce s the hypothesis that MB is characterized by a 470 

physiological and neural profile of modified arousal. This point is here supported by the 471 

significantly higher GS magnitude during sMB compared to content -full states , such as MW. 472 

Given that high GS amplitude is a robust marker of low cortical arousal (Liu et al., 2017; Wong et 473 

al., 2013) , these findings add to previous findings that MB ’s brain correlates resemble those of 474 

sleep -like activity , as previously supported by the detection of local slow wave activity during MB 475 

episodes (Andrillon et al., 2021; Munoz -Musat et al., 2025) . Interestingly, the association with low 476 

arousal markers appeared more robust in the self -caught reports. This pattern may point toward 477 

two distinct mechanisms. On the one hand, it may stem f rom the nature of the task : as participants 478 

continuously monitor their cognition, they may more readily identify a MB episode whenever they 479 

perceive a relevant decrease in vigilance. On the other hand, the task itself may actively induce 480

It is important to note , though , that sleep and MB are not the same state . Phenomenologically, 483 

individuals distinguish the feeling of an empty mind from the sensation of drowsiness, and 484 

reporting frequencies of MB and sleep do not correlate (Boulakis et al., 2025) . As a limitation, i n485 

our protocol, w hile participants were explicitly instructed to report MB, they were not offered the 486 

choice of “Sleep”, which could have facilitated a clearer interpretation of the underlying 487 

mechanism of the two MB capturing methods .488 

What we found, though, was that pMB repor ts did not significantly differ in their GS magnitude 489 

levels compared to other mental states and were also associated with a FC pattern characterized 490 

by anticorrelations between the DMN and sensory networks (Pattern 2), for which MW was also 491 

associated with. Th ese finding may arise from the fact that probe -caught tasks may capture a 492 

boarder, more heterogeneous range of blanks (Chu et al., 2023) . For instance, pMB often 493 

aggregates both the introspective experience of an empty mind and the retrospective inability to 494 

retrieve previous thoughts (Boulakis et al., 2026) . This aligns with our recent study in the context 495 

of an attention task, where pMB was associated not only with the hyperconnected brain FC pattern, 496 

but also with an anticorrelation FC pattern, and this effect was mediated by perceived levels of 497 

vigilance : when people reported being alert, the connectomes around MB reports resembled the 498 

hyperconnected pattern (Boulakis, Kusztor, et al., 2025) . Overall, the broad associations between 499 

pMB with distinct FC configurations may reflect the different kinds of experience and 500 

psychological mechanisms that a re grouped under the umbrella term MB (Boulakis et al., 2026; 501 

Boulakis & Demertzi, 2025) .502 

This study presents some limitation s. A primary drawback is our relatively small sample size 503 

(n=22), although this is comparable to other MB studies (Adam et al., 2020; Torres et al., 2024) .504 

Notably , the activation analysis could only be performed on a subset of participants who reported 505 

MB episodes in both tasks, effectively reducing the sample size to n=13, a challenge inherent to 506 

MB low report frequency. Furthermore, as an art ifact of the different methodologies , the MB 507 

window of analysis differed slightly between tasks. For the self -caught task, MB was defined 508 

immediately preceding the button press, whereas for the probe -caught task, the mental state was 509 

defined prior to the probe appearance. This inevitably places sMB reports in closer temporal 510

Conclusion s516 

Our findings demonstrate that sMB resembles pMB in their behavioral properties and brain 517 

correlates of hyperconnectivity, although they diverge in the recruitment of the dACC , a difference 518 

we attribute to the meta -cognitive process involved inherent to the self -caught task . This positions 519 

the self -caught methodology as an adequate tool to detect MB episodes. Therefore, this work 520 

provides a new framework for studying MB, enabling new experimental protocols, where probe -521 

caught approaches might be disrupting the stream of thought. Overall, our work advances our 522 

ability to investigate content -less experiences that are of both theoretical and clinical relevance. 523
