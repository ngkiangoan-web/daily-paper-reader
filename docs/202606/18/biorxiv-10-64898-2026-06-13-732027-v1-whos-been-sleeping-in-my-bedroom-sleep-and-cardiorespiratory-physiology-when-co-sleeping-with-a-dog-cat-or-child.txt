15 Abstract 

16 Co-sleeping with pets or children is common, yet its effects on sleep, cardiorespiratory 17 physiology, and behavioral outcomes are understudied. We examined within-person associations 18 between co-sleeping with a dog, cat, or child and sleep characteristics, measures of 19 cardiorespiratory physiology, and next-day physical activity in 1,649,083 person-days from 20 11,733 adults wearing the WHOOP wearable device. Participants reported nightly co-sleeping 21 via a daily journal in the device’s companion smartphone application, and linear mixed-effects 22 models compared nights with and without co-sleeping within the same individual. Co-sleeping 23 was associated with modest improvements in cardiorespiratory physiology, including lower 24 resting heart rate (0.8-1.1 beats/min), lower respiratory rate (0.04-0.07 breaths/min), and higher 25 heart rate variability (1.41-1.95 ms). Sleep outcomes were mixed, with longer sleep duration 26 (5.5-9.6 min) but more disturbances (0.36-0.40 instances) and slightly less restorative sleep 27 (0.23-1.17%). Associations were generally consistent across groups, although child co-sleeping 28 showed greater sleep disruption. Next-day physical activity was higher following dog and cat co-29 sleeping (7.6 and 7.4 intensity-weighted min, respectively) but lower following child co-sleeping 30 (2.6 intensity-weighted min). Although effect sizes were small (β range: 0.008–0.045), findings 31 suggest that co-sleeping is associated with a trade-off between modest cardiorespiratory benefits 32 and mild sleep disruption, indicating that co-sleeping decisions may be driven more by personal 33 and contextual factors than by concerns about physiologic impact. 34 35 Keywords: sleep quality, wearable, pet, autonomic function 36

37 Introduction 

38 Sleep is essential for overall health and both physical and cognitive function [1], yet a 39 significant proportion of adults in Westernized societies have insufficient or low-quality sleep, 40 with roughly one-third sleeping less than 7 hours per day [2] and nearly ~30% reporting 41 nighttime sleeping difficulties [3]. With the high prevalence of inadequate sleep, it is important 42 to determine factors that influence sleep quantity and quality. Because sleep occurs within a 43 broader social and environmental context, identifying factors that influence sleep quantity and 44 quality, including sleeping arrangements, is an important area of inquiry. Co-sleeping is 45 common, with 30-50% of adults sharing a bed with a child, pet or both [4, 5]. Despite the 46 common practice of co-sleeping, the physiologic consequences of these arrangements remain 47 incompletely understood. 48 A recent review by Andre et al. [6] provides a comprehensive overview of co-sleeping 49 research. Co-sleeping with a child shows a mismatch between improved subjective (i.e., self-50 report) sleep and more disrupted objective (i.e., polysomnography or device-based) sleep 51 measures, especially among mothers. When co-sleeping with animals, species-specific sleep 52 patterns may influence human sleep. Dogs are polyphasic and typically experience several sleep-53 wake transitions during the night, whereas cats are crepuscular and tend to be more active in the 54 evening and early morning, when humans are more typically asleep [7-9]. Additional factors 55 such as movement and allergen exposure may further contribute to sleep disruption [10, 11], 56 though pets may provide security or other psychological benefits that could improve sleep 57 quality [6, 12]. Empirical studies reflect this balance of competing influences. Hoffman et al. 58 [13] reported that nighttime dog movements were associated with increased movement in human 59 bed partners. Similarly, Patel et al. [14] found poorer sleep quality when dogs shared the bed 60 compared to being present in the room. Although fewer studies have examined cat co-sleeping,

61 available evidence suggests that self-reported sleep disruption may be similar for cat and dog 62 owners [15]. 63 Despite growing interest in co-sleeping, much of the existing literature is limited by small 64 sample sizes, reliance on self-reported sleep outcomes, and between-person designs that compare 65 habitual co-sleepers and non-co-sleepers rather than examining within-person differences. Such 66 designs risk confounding if the two groups differ systematically in demographics, sleep habits, or 67 other lifestyle factors. Moreover, even less is known regarding how co-sleeping relates to 68 cardiorespiratory markers such as resting heart rate (RHR) and heart rate variability (HRV). Poor 69 sleep is associated with elevated RHR and lower HRV [16, 17], suggesting that co-sleeping 70 could influence these measures, but this has not been directly examined at scale. 71 Large-scale, passively collected wearable data offer new opportunities to address these 72 gaps. Accordingly, we examined within-person associations between co-sleeping with a dog, cat, 73 or child and nightly sleep outcomes, measures of cardiorespiratory physiology, and next-day 74 physical activity in a large sample of wearable device users. 75 Materials and methods 

76 Study design and participants 

77 This study was a retrospective cohort analysis using data from adult users (18-79 yrs) of a 78 wearable fitness tracker (WHOOP Inc., Boston, MA), designed, conducted, and reported in 79 accordance with the Strengthening the Reporting of Observational Studies in Epidemiology 80 (STROBE) guidelines for cohort studies [18]. Participants were randomly selected from the full 81 database if they met predefined inclusion criteria designed to ensure both data completeness, 82 similar between-group demographics, and within-person variability. Eligible users had at least 14 83 days (and up to 365) of continuous data in 2024, with no missing sleep or biometric measures.

107 photoplethysmography signal to estimate HRV, calculated as the root mean square of successive 108 differences (RMSSD). To generate nightly RHR and HRV values, WHOOP first filtered out 109 epochs identified as wake or as having low signal quality. The remaining epochs were then 110 aggregated using a weighted average, in which epochs with higher likelihood of SWS and 111 epochs closer to the end of the sleep were assigned higher weights. Sleep duration was defined as 112 total sleep time (light, SWS, REM). Restorative sleep was defined as the sum of time spent in 113 SWS and REM sleep. WHOOP has been validated against electrocardiography and 114 polysomnography for heart rate (99% agreement) and 2-stage sleep categorization (86-89% 115 agreement) [19, 20]. 116 Daily physical activity was detected automatically using heart rate and motion data from 117 the WHOOP strap or logged manually on the WHOOP smartphone application by the 118 participant. Maximum heart rate was estimated using the non-linear formula 192–0.007*age² 119 [21] manually set, or defined as the highest recorded heart rate during an exercise period with 120 high signal quality. Continuous heart rate data during each activity were used to calculate time 121 spent in five zones defined as percentages of heart rate maximum: zone 1 (50-60%), zone 2 (60-122 70%), zone 3 (70-80%), zone 4 (80-90%), and zone 5 (90-100%). Minutes in each zone were 123 multiplied by zone-specific weights (i.e., zone 1=1, zone 2=2, etc.), then summed to yield a 124 composite, summated heart rate zone score (SHRZS), which we refer to as physical activity (i.e., 125 intensity-weighted min) [22]. In November 2024, the platform transitioned to heart rate reserve-126 based zones; as discussed later, we performed a sensitivity analysis using data from prior to this 127 transition (January-October) and found that inclusion of November and December data did not 128 change study outcomes.

129 Self-reported co-sleeping with dog, cat, or child 

130 Co-sleep behavior was self-reported via the WHOOP smartphone application’s daily 131 journal feature. Each morning, users were prompted to answer the following yes/no questions 132 regarding the prior night: “Had a dog in the room while sleeping?”, “Had a cat in the room while 133 sleeping?”, and “Slept with a child in your bedroom?”. Users could also skip any question. Only 134 days with explicit “yes” or “no” responses were included in the primary analysis. Journal data 135 were used to identify nights with and without co-sleeping exposure for each group. 136 Statistical analysis 

137 All analyses were conducted using day-level physiological and behavioral data for 138 individuals reporting co-sleeping with a dog, cat, or child. Prior to modeling, data were 139 processed to isolate nights with complete sleep, biometric, and journal responses. 140 To account for differences in demographic and physiological characteristics across the 141 three co-sleeping groups, a matching procedure was applied. Because the child co-sleeping group 142 had the fewest individuals, it served as the reference population. Individuals in the dog and cat 143 groups were matched 1:1 to participants in the child group using nearest-neighbor Mahalanobis 144 distance [23]. Matching variables included age, sex, BMI, and person-level means of RHR, 145 HRV, sleep duration, and physical activity. This yielded three approximately balanced groups of 146 equal size, minimizing the potential effects of these variables on our analysis ( S1 Table ). 147 The primary outcomes of interest included nocturnal RHR and HRV, respiratory rate 148 (RR), total sleep duration, number of disturbances, a disturbance index expressed as disturbances 149 per hour of sleep, restorative sleep percentage (SWS + REM), and next-day physical activity. For 150 each exposure, nightly deviations (within-person level) and person-mean values (between-person 151 level) were calculated to model chronic and acute associations. Given the study aim of

239 Sex moderated several co-sleeping associations 

240 Co-sleeping effects differed by sex for several outcomes ( S5 Table ). 260 Findings were robust to sensitivity checks 

261 Two sensitivity analyses were conducted to assess robustness of the primary within-262 person findings to the handling of nights with missing co-sleeping responses (dog: ~31%, cat: 263 ~33%, child: ~56%). Sensitivity analysis A ( S8 Table ) compared the primary complete-case 264 approach to treating null responses as no co-sleeping (null-as-no) across all groups and 265 outcomes, and the model also included a null-response indicator as a direct covariate alongside 266 the primary model terms across all groups and outcomes. Effect directions and statistical 267 significance were fully preserved across all 24 within-person outcomes, supporting the 268 robustness of primary findings. Sensitivity analysis B ( S9 Table ) applied multiple imputation by 269 chained equations (MICE; m=5 imputations) across all groups and outcomes, finding that all 24 270 within-person effects remained statistically significant after pooling. 271 Effect sizes were small 

272 To assess the practical magnitudes of the observed within-person associations, effect 273 sizes for within-person co-sleeping associations were calculated (absolute value of the 274 standardized β range: 0.008-0.045; data not shown). The largest effects were observed for dog 275 co-sleeping on resting heart rate (β = -0.045) and sleep duration (β = 0.043) and for child co-276 sleeping on restorative sleep percentage (β = -0.045), with similar effects observed across other 277 outcomes and co-sleeping conditions. 278 Discussion 

279 We evaluated the within-person effects of co-sleeping with a dog, cat, or child on sleep, 280 cardiorespiratory physiology, and next-day physical activity using a wearable device. Co-281 sleeping was associated with modest improvements in cardiorespiratory physiology, including a 282 decreased RHR, increased HRV, and decreased RR concurrent with increased total sleep time

Although the combination of improved 285 cardiorespiratory physiology and more disrupted sleep may appear paradoxical, co-sleeping may 286 simultaneously enhance emotional comfort or perceived safety, which has been linked to 287 improved HRV profiles [29-31], while simultaneously introducing movement and disrupting 288 sleep. That the small magnitude of sleep disruption did not appear to offset the cardiorespiratory 289 associations is consistent with these being driven by distinct pathways. 290 Child co-sleeping showed a more disruptive pattern than pet co-sleeping. Child co-291 sleeping was associated with smaller increases in sleep duration and greater increases in 292 disturbances and more substantially reduced restorative sleep percentage relative to pet co-293 sleeping. Between-person effects in the child group were consistent with these trends and 294 persisted after covariate adjustment, suggesting that co-sleeping with children may be less 295 voluntary and more context-dependent than co-sleeping with pets. Prior work has shown that co-296 sleeping in children is often influenced by caregiving needs, developmental transitions, or family 297 circumstances rather than preference alone [32], and is associated with increased nighttime 298 awakenings, greater caregiver involvement, and poorer sleep indices [33, 34]. 299 Next-day physical activity increased following co-sleeping with a pet (with similar 300 magnitudes in dogs and cats) but decreased in days following co-sleeping with a child. Past 301 research has shown that dog ownership is associated with increased activity levels [35, 36], 302 whereas little research has directly examined cat ownership and physical activity. Decreased 303 activity following co-sleeping with children may reflect fatigue or constrained discretionary time 304 to be active [37, 38]. For example, co-sleeping with a child may limit opportunities for early-305 morning exercise if caregivers wake simultaneously with the child. These findings suggest that

However, as with the overall analysis, sex-stratified effects were very small. 314 Our study has several important strengths including a large sample, a within-person 315 design that reduces confounding from between-person differences, a careful matching procedure 316 that yielded similar demographic and behavioral factors across the three co-sleeping groups, and 317 objective measurement of study outcomes with the wearable device. However, our study also 318 had several caveats worth noting. The Mahalanobis matching resulted in highly comparable 319 groups, although the SMDs do indicate a slightly larger difference in physical activity levels, 320 with the child group less active than the dog and cat groups. Next, co-sleeping was self-reported 321 and a substantial portion of nights had missing journal responses (~31% for dog, ~33% for cat, 322 ~56% for child), though sensitivity analyses confirmed that within-person findings were robust 323 across all outcomes. The WHOOP journal does not distinguish between bed-sharing and room-324 sharing (e.g., pet was on the floor, or child was in a bassinet), nor does it capture potentially 325 important factors such as bed size, number of co-sleepers, pet size, or child age. Finally, while 326 the WHOOP device provides an objective measure for outcome assessment and has shown good 327 validity for heart rate outcomes (e.g., RHR and HRV) [20, 40], wearable-based measures of 328 sleep architecture, while scalable, do not achieve the accuracy of polysomnography [19, 41, 42].

329 Conclusions 

330 Co-sleeping with a pet or child was associated with modest but consistent changes in 331 cardiorespiratory physiology and sleep, reflecting a trade-off between potential calming or 332 comfort-related effects and increased sleep fragmentation. Patterns were broadly similar across 333 companion types, though child co-sleeping showed more pronounced sleep disruption, 334 particularly in females. Effect sizes were small, suggesting that co-sleeping decisions may be 335 guided more by personal factors such as comfort, caregiving needs, and cultural or family 336 routines than by concerns about large impacts on sleep or cardiorespiratory function. 337
