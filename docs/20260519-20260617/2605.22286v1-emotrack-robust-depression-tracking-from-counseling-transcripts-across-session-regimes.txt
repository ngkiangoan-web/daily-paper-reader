## Abstract 

Text-based counseling is an important interface for AI mental-health support, where transcripts may be used to monitor depression severity and flag sessions requiring timely human review. However, robust PHQ-8 prediction across session regimes remains challenging: fine-tuning-based methods can exploit richer supervision but may generalize poorly under data scarcity, while prompt-based LLM methods are data-efficient but usually treat each transcript holistically and provide limited support for longitudinal context. We study robust depression tracking from coun-seling transcripts across single-session and multi-session regimes. We introduce LONG COUNSEL -8, a multi-session counseling dataset with session-level PHQ-8 supervision for evaluating repeated-session tracking under partial symptom dis-closure and cross-session continuity. We further propose EmoTrack , a PHQ-8 prediction framework that combines LLM-extracted clinical signals with frozen turn-level semantic embeddings and trains symptom-specific predictors over the resulting transcript representation. When prior sessions are available, EmoTrack can further incorporate them through compact cross-session memory. Experiments on L ONG COUNSEL -8 and D AIC -W OZ show that EmoTrack achieves a clear gain on the real single-session benchmark, including a 13.5% relative MAE reduction over the strongest D AIC -W OZ baseline, and remains competitive with the strongest longitudinal baseline on L ONG COUNSEL -8. 

## 1 Introduction 

Text-based counseling is becoming an important interface for AI mental-health support, as reflected by recent counseling-oriented dialogue systems and datasets [ 16 , 26 , 38 ]. In such settings, depression assessment is not only an offline prediction problem: a system may need to estimate the client’s current PHQ-8 [ 11 ] severity during or after a counseling conversation, flag high-risk sessions when the predicted score exceeds a predefined threshold, and support timely human review. We study this problem across session regimes, including single-session settings, where only the current transcript is available, and multi-session settings, where prior counseling sessions from the same client may provide additional context. Here, a turn refers to a single utterance in a counseling dialogue, produced by either the client or the counselor, and a session refers to one complete counseling encounter consisting of multiple turns. Existing text-based depression assessment methods mainly follow two directions. The first uses trainable neural or fine-tuning-based models to learn task-specific predictors from labeled data [ 13 ,22 , 29 ]. The second uses prompt-based or holistic LLM methods to directly score a transcript, extract clinical features, or complete structured questionnaires [ 14 , 30 ]. These two directions reflect different assumptions about supervision: the former relies on task-specific labeled data, while the latter relies more on the general clinical reasoning ability of LLMs.

However, these methods face a two-sided cross-regime limitation. Fine-tuning-based methods can benefit from data-rich multi-session settings, but may generalize poorly when labeled data are scarce or the target regime changes. Prompt-based holistic methods are more data-efficient and can work better in scarce-data settings, but they usually treat each transcript as a whole and provide limited mechanisms for learning turn-level evidence or exploiting cross-session continuity. The key problem is therefore not only longitudinal tracking, but robust PHQ-8 prediction across session regimes. To address these limitations, we pursue a hybrid and regime-adaptive approach. Rather than using LLM prompting as the final zero-shot predictor, we use it only to extract structured clinical cues; rather than fine-tuning a large encoder on scarce depression labels, we train supervised predictors on top of frozen semantic representations. The goal is to build a strong current-session model that remains effective in single-session settings, while allowing prior-session context to be added when available. Realizing this idea raises three challenges: (i) depression evidence is distributed across turns and often expressed indirectly; (ii) prior-session context is useful but noisy, so direct history concatenation can introduce stale or irrelevant evidence; and (iii) existing resources are insufficient for evaluating both regimes, since D AIC -W OZ provides real single-session interviews [ 7], while existing multi-session counseling resources rarely provide standardized session-level PHQ supervision [24, 26, 34]. We propose EmoTrack , a PHQ-8 prediction framework, together with L ONG COUNSEL -8, a multi-session counseling dataset, to address these challenges. (i) For distributed and indirect within-session evidence, EmoTrack represents each session as structured clinical-feature tokens and turn-level semantic tokens, then uses self-attention to integrate heterogeneous evidence and PHQ-8 symptom queries to attend to symptom-relevant cues. (ii) For noisy prior-session context, EmoTrack avoids direct history concatenation and instead compresses the previous session into compact attention-based memory, which is selectively injected through gated symptom-level updates and skipped when no history is available. (iii) For the evaluation gap, we construct L ONG COUNSEL -8, which provides session-level PHQ-8 labels for multi-session counseling conversations, enabling evaluation under partial symptom disclosure and multi-session settings. Our contributions are threefold: (1) we frame PHQ-8 prediction across single- and multi-session counseling settings and propose EmoTrack, a practical model that combines structured clinical signals, turn-level semantics, and compact prior-session memory; (2) we introduce L ONG COUNSEL -8, a controlled synthetic benchmark with known symptom trajectories, partial disclosure, cross-session continuity, and standardized session-level PHQ-8 supervision; and (3) we evaluate EmoTrack on LONG COUNSEL -8 and D AIC -W OZ [7], showing a clear gain on the real single-session benchmark and competitive performance on the longitudinal benchmark. 

## 2 Related Work 

Text-based depression-score prediction. Recent AI mental-health support and counseling systems are predominantly built around text-based conversation, as reflected by resources and systems such as ChatCounselor [ 16 ], PsyDial [ 26 ], and MentalChat16K [ 38 ]. We therefore focus on depression assessment from dialogue transcripts, and specifically on score-oriented symptom prediction rather than coarse screening labels. Prior score-based methods largely follow the two directions introduced above (Table 1). Trainable neural or fine-tuning-based methods learn task-specific predictors from labeled data, including RoBERTa- or sentence-embedding models for PHQ-8 prediction [ 13 ], hier-archical attention models for PHQ symptom prediction [ 22 ], and symptom-retrieval architectures adapted from questionnaire-score prediction [ 29 ]. These methods can exploit labeled supervision, but are typically developed and evaluated in single-session settings and can be sensitive to the target data regime. Prompt-based or holistic LLM methods instead use the general reasoning ability of LLMs to analyze a transcript, complete questionnaires, or extract clinical features [ 2, 14 , 17 , 21 , 30 , 39 ]. These approaches are more data-efficient, but they usually treat the transcript as a whole and provide limited mechanisms for learning turn-level evidence or selectively using prior-session context. Overall, existing score-oriented methods remain mostly snapshot-oriented, leaving PHQ-8 prediction across single- and multi-session regimes underexplored. Counseling data for cross-regime evaluation. The second gap raised in the introduction is evaluative: existing resources rarely support both real single-session PHQ prediction and longitudinal counseling-style PHQ tracking. D AIC -W OZ [7 ] remains the most widely used real PHQ-8 interview benchmark in this line of work [ 2, 5 , 13 , 14 , 30 ], but it contains only single-session virtual-agent interviews. In contrast, counseling resources derived from demonstration videos, expert role-play, reconstructed dialogues, licensed therapy transcripts, or LLM generation provide richer counseling-style interactions [ 6, 24 , 26 , 34 ], and some include multi-session structure. However, these corpora generally lack standardized per-session PHQ supervision. As a result, existing datasets cover either real single-session PHQ assessment or multi-session counseling dialogue, but not both standardized symptom trajectories and repeated counseling sessions for the same client. L ONG COUNSEL -8 is designed to fill this evaluation gap by pairing coherent cross-session counseling trajectories with aligned PHQ-8 symptom supervision at each session, while D AIC -W OZ serves as the real single-session anchor. ## 3 Problem Formulation 

We study PHQ-8 depression tracking from counseling conversations in a cross-regime setting, covering both single-session settings, where only the current transcript is available, and multi-session settings, where prior counseling sessions from the same client may provide additional context. Following the terminology introduced above, a turn is a single utterance within a counseling conversation, and a session is one complete counseling encounter. Intra-session modeling concerns turn-level interaction within the current session, while cross-session modeling concerns dependencies between the current session and prior sessions from the same client. Given the transcript of the current session and, when available, prior-session context, the goal is to predict the client’s PHQ-8 depression severity at the current session. 

Clinical target. Depression severity is measured by the widely used PHQ-8 [ 11 ], which contains 

J=8 symptoms assessing anhedonia, depressed mood, sleep disturbance, fatigue, appetite disturbance, low self-worth or guilt, concentration difficulty, and psychomotor change. Each symptom is scored on a four-point ordinal scale yj ∈ { 0, 1, 2, 3}, and the total score is Y = PJj=1 yj ∈ [0 , 24] .

Task definition. Given the current counseling transcript T (t) at session t and optional prior-session context H(t−1) , the task is to predict the client’s PHQ-8 severity at the current session. Here, H(t−1) denotes the set of all previous-session counseling transcripts for the same client, i.e., 

H(t−1) = {T (1) , T (2) , . . . , T (t−1) }, which is empty when t = 1 . This formulation allows access to all prior sessions from the same client; in EmoTrack, we instantiate this setting with the immediately previous session as the memory source, since it provides the most recent longitudinal context while avoiding the noise and cost of full-history modeling. ## 4 Approach: EmoTrack 

We propose EmoTrack , a transformer-based PHQ-8 predictor for counseling transcripts in single-and multi-session settings. For the current session, EmoTrack builds two client-side token sequences: LLM-extracted clinical-feature tokens and turn-level dialogue embeddings (Section 4.1). An encoder– decoder backbone integrates both sources and uses learned PHQ-8 symptom queries to read out symptom-specific representations (Section 4.2), which a prediction head maps to per-symptom severities with an aggregate-plus-symptom-wise objective (Section 4.3). When history is available, a compact memory module selectively refines these representations with previous-session evidence (Section 4.4). Figure 1 summarizes the pipeline, with formal Algorithm 1 in Appendix B. 

4.1 Input Representation 

Given the client-side current-session transcript T (t), EmoTrack constructs two complementary token sequences, one from structured clinical features and the other from client turns; we focus on client turns because they yield the most informative dialogue view for PHQ-8 prediction, while counselor-only and combined views perform worse (Figure 2c). We extract F =23 clinician-style feature scores from T (t) by prompting an LLM with the AIDA feature schema [ 14 ], organized into three clinically motivated groups (8 PHQ-aligned symptom indicators, 5 linguistic-pattern features, 10 cognitive-distortion markers); each score is rated on a [0 , 10] scale and z-normalized to {zi}Fi=1 

using training-split statistics. The two sources are complementary, and removing either consistently degrades performance on both benchmarks (Figure 2b).

4.2 Encoder–Decoder Backbone 

The encoder–decoder backbone takes the clinical tokens Hc and client-turn tokens S as input and produces one latent representation for each PHQ-8 symptom. We first concatenate the two token sequences into a single current-session memory, X = [ Hc; S] ∈

R(F +N )×d. A padding mask is applied so that self-attention ignores padded client turns. The concatenated sequence is then processed by Transformer encoder layers. In each encoder layer, X

is projected into queries, keys, and values, and self-attention updates each token by attending to all other clinical and client-turn tokens in the current session. This yields a contextualized representation in which each token aggregates relevant evidence from both branches. We use Lenc =2 encoder layers and Ldec =4 decoder layers, selected on validation MAE; this moderate depth is competitive with both shallower and deeper variants on the two benchmarks (Appendix E.2). Replacing the EmoTrack predictor with standard regressors over the same features substantially worsens PHQ-8 prediction, supporting the attention-based backbone (Table 3). 

PHQ-symptom query decoder. EmoTrack uses J=8 learned query vectors, one for each PHQ-8 symptom, collected in Q ∈ RJ×d. In the decoder, these symptom queries cross-attend to the encoder output X, allowing each symptom to retrieve its own relevant evidence from the current-session tokens. The decoder returns symptom representations D ∈ RJ×d, where each row corresponds to one PHQ-8 symptom. Appendix E.8 compares this design with a mean-pooled encoder head that first compresses the transcript into a single session vector.

## 5 Evaluation: L ONG COUNSEL -8 

Existing datasets do not support controlled evaluation of longitudinal PHQ-8 tracking in counsel-ing: real counseling resources are scarce, mostly single-session, and rarely provide standardized per-session PHQ supervision [ 7 , 34 ], while synthetic counseling corpora lack known longitudinal symptom trajectories [ 10 , 15 , 24 ]. We therefore construct L ONG COUNSEL -8, a synthetic longitudinal counseling corpus in which each client returns across five visits with prescribed hidden PHQ-8 states; Appendix C provides the construction and evaluation details. 

Data Generation. A longitudinal counseling corpus must specify a connected generation chain: (1) who the client is, (2) how the client’s overall depression severity changes across visits, (3) how each visit-level PHQ-8 total score is decomposed into eight symptom-level scores, and (4) how these symptom scores are expressed in counseling dialogue. LONG COUNSEL -8 grounds every step in empirical sources rather than arbitrary prompts: (1) we instantiate client backgrounds, presenting concerns, and session-stage structures from PsychEval-derived cases [ 24 ]; (2) we reconstruct five-visit PHQ-8 total-score trajectories from PSYCHE-D [ 19 ]so that longitudinal change follows empirical depression patterns; (3) we decompose each visit-level total into an eight-dimensional PHQ symptom profile using NHANES DPQ responses [ 4]; and (4) we translate each symptom score into private client behaviors that determine what symptom evidence appears in dialogue. RealCBT counseling conversations [ 34 ] then calibrate how this evidence is expressed, aligning generated turns with real-dialogue statistics over speaker role, counseling stage, utterance length, question form, opening pattern, and lexical overlap. 

Evaluation target and corpus. Because real depression datasets only provide questionnaire-derived labels, we adopt a realistic protocol: models are trained on simulated self-report PHQ-8 scores but evaluated against the latent PHQ-8 state that drives dialogue generation. The latent values appear only as private generation conditions, so models must infer symptom severity from conversational evidence. After validity filtering, L ONG COUNSEL -8 contains 3,599 trajectories (mostly five labeled sessions per trajectory, with some contributing 1–4 labeled sessions due to generation interruptions; 17,122 labeled sessions in total) covering stable, improving, worsening, and fluctuating symptom courses. Audits along trajectory diversity, self-report-to-latent fidelity, and dialogue realism against 6RealCBT and synthetic counseling baselines [ 10 , 15 , 34 ] support the benchmark validity: final cue-bank pruning improves item exact match from 0.77 to 0.85 and reduces total MAE from 1.69 to 

1.21 , while L ONG COUNSEL -8 is closest to RealCBT under both judge-based indistinguishability and psycholinguistic distance. L ONG COUNSEL -8 is designed for controlled benchmark evaluation rather than clinical validation; real longitudinal clinical validation remains future work. Detailed construction prompts, fidelity checks, and similarity analyses are provided in Appendix C. 

## 6 Experiment 

This section evaluates EmoTrack in multi- and single-session settings. We describe the setup in §6.1, with implementation and training details in Appendix D; compare against baselines in §6.2; and analyze input-source, speaker-view, and predictor-architecture ablations in §6.3. Appendix E complements these results with cross-dataset transfer (§E.1) and seven ablations covering encoder– decoder depth (§E.2), history dropout (§E.3), maximum context length (§E.4), memory mechanism (§E.5), memory capacity (§E.6), the symptom-wise auxiliary loss (§E.7), and the PHQ-symptom query decoder (§E.8), together with inference-time comparisons (§E.9). 

6.1 Experiment Settings Datasets. We evaluate on two depression-assessment benchmarks. For L ONG COUNSEL -8, we use the cleaned 3,599 principle-guided synthetic multi-session counseling trajectories (17,122 valid self-report-labeled sessions) constructed to cover diverse severity ranges, progression patterns, and degrees of history dependence under standardized PHQ-8 supervision. D AIC -W OZ [7 ] contains 189 real single-session clinical interviews with self-reported PHQ-8 labels. D AIC -W OZ uses the official participant-level split of 107 train, 35 validation, and 47 test interviews; L ONG COUNSEL -8 is split by profile–trend trajectories into 80% train/validation and 20% test. 

Models. All utterance embeddings are extracted using a frozen embedding model (default: Qwen3-Embedding-8B [ 28 ]), which produces 4,096-dimensional vectors for each utterance. The 23 AIDA clinical feature scores are extracted by prompting Qwen3.5-35B-A3B [ 28 ] with structured scoring rubrics. Each extraction run uses a distinct random seed, and all downstream models are trained and evaluated on matched-seed AIDA features to ensure a fair comparison. 

Evaluation. All results are reported as 5-seed means ± standard deviation. The primary metric is mean absolute error (MAE) on the total PHQ-8 score against ground-truth labels. We additionally report per-session MAE on L ONG COUNSEL -8 to assess longitudinal tracking ability. 

Baselines. We include state-of-the-art baselines spanning LLM-based clinical feature extraction, LLM questionnaire completion, neural text encoding, and symptom-level depression-score prediction. • AIDA [14 ]: extracts 23 LLM-rated clinical features covering PHQ-aligned symptoms, linguistic patterns, and cognitive distortions, and fits a ridge regressor. • LMIQ [ 30 ]: prompts an LLM to complete structured mental-health questionnaires on behalf of the participant, and trains a random forest on the resulting questionnaire features. • Lau et al. [13 ]: uses a parameter-efficiently tuned RoBERTa question–response encoder with a sentence-transformer branch for depression-severity prediction. • Milintsevich et al. [ 22 ]: predicts PHQ-8 symptom scores with a hierarchical attention model over sentence embeddings, and sums symptom predictions into the total PHQ-8 score. • EnsemBERT [ 29 ]: retrieves symptom-relevant utterances and predicts each questionnaire symptom with attention over utterance, symptom-description, and response-choice embeddings. We adapt it from BDI-II prediction to PHQ-8 symptom prediction. • Self-report : (L ONG COUNSEL -8 only) uses the client’s own PHQ-8 self-assessment as the estimate. For LLM-feature and questionnaire-completion baselines [ 14 , 30 ], we report two history variants: 

now , which uses only the current session, and all , which concatenates the current and prior sessions. For embedding-based neural baselines [ 13 , 22 , 29 ], we report both the original encoder setting and the Qwen-embedding variant where applicable. 76.2 Performance Comparison 

Figure 2a summarizes overall MAE on both benchmarks, Figure 3 gives the full L ONG COUNSEL -8 comparison, and Table 2 reports detailed D AIC -W OZ results. The results support three main findings. First, EmoTrack achieves a clear gain on D AIC -W OZ and remains competitive with the strongest longitudinal baseline on L ONG COUNSEL -8. On D AIC -W OZ , it reduces MAE from AIDA’s 2.8234 ±0.2099 to 2.4434 ±0.1404 , a relative reduction of 13 .5% . On L ONG COUNSEL -8, its MAE is close to Lau et al. ( 2.65 vs. 2.67 ), so we interpret the longitudinal result as competitive rather than as a large-margin win. Second, EmoTrack has stronger cross-regime performance than baselines that specialize in one setting. Lau et al. is close to EmoTrack on L ONG COUNSEL -8 but performs much worse on D AIC -W OZ (4.4367 , or 4.0927 with Qwen embeddings), while AIDA is the strongest baseline on D AIC -W OZ but is less effective on L ONG COUNSEL -8 ( 3.37 ). Third, memory helps after the first session. EmoTrack is not the best in the first session, where no history is available, but gradually overtakes baselines in later sessions, indicating that compact previous-session memory provides useful longitudinal context. ## 7 Conclusion 

We presented EmoTrack, a framework for PHQ-8 prediction across single- and multi-session counsel-ing transcripts, together with L ONG COUNSEL -8, a benchmark for longitudinal depression tracking. ## C Construction and Evaluation of L ONG COUNSEL -8 

LONG COUNSEL -8 is constructed as a controlled longitudinal counseling corpus for session-level PHQ-8 tracking. The construction is organized as a traceable generation chain: (1) define a stable client profile and session plan, (2) assign a five-visit depression trajectory, (3) decompose each visit-level severity anchor into eight PHQ-8 symptom scores, and (4) render those private symptom states as natural counseling dialogue. Each step is anchored in empirical sources or deterministic validation rules, so the resulting benchmark is not driven by unconstrained prompting. 

C.1 Construction Generation chain. Each trajectory contains five counseling sessions for one client. For session t,the latent symptom state is a symptom-level PHQ-8 vector

∈ [0 , 3] , (6) whose sum gives the visit-level PHQ-8 total score. PsychEval-derived cases [ 24 ] provide stable client backgrounds, presenting concerns, and session-stage structures. PSYCHE-D [ 19 ] provides the longitudinal severity anchors, ensuring that the five-visit changes follow empirical depression trajectories rather than prompt-selected trends. NHANES DPQ responses [ 4] then provide observed symptom co-occurrence patterns: for each anchor, we sample a complete PHQ-style response vector and retain the first eight symptoms as the latent PHQ-8 state. The client-profile step is a deterministic conversion of PsychEval-derived cases rather than a free-form client-invention prompt. It preserves stable identity, biography, presenting concern, topic, growth history, beliefs, and target behaviors, while removing counselor-only administrative material and process instructions. Session themes are rewritten as client-side disclosure beats that specify a 15 concrete event, bodily or contextual triggers, feared meaning, avoidance cost, and desired help. Profiles without valid client information or session plans are removed before trajectory assignment. For trajectory reconstruction, PSYCHE-D quarterly observations are chained into five anchors: the first visit uses the first available start score, and subsequent visits use the corresponding quarter-end scores. When adjacent quarterly windows disagree on a shared boundary, the two boundary values are averaged and rounded. For each reconstructed PHQ-9 anchor, NHANES DPQ rows with complete symptom responses are grouped by total score. A deterministic hash of the global seed, participant id, visit index, and anchor score selects one matching response vector. The first eight PHQ symptoms form the PHQ-8 vector, and their sum becomes the latent PHQ-8 total. The decomposition uses exact observed NHANES total-score buckets, with all-zero and all-maximum anchors handled as deterministic boundary cases. 

Condensed prompt templates. The following templates are used only after deterministic profile and trajectory construction. The labels name the generation stage; they are not part of the prompt text. Bracketed spans denote instance-specific content instantiated from one client’s profile, one session’s private symptom cues, the public dialogue history, retrieved memories, and the current turn plan. 

Behavior-cue generation. 

> Given a PHQ-8 symptom, an ordinal severity level, and the intended direction when ap-plicable, write short candidate behavioral cues that could naturally appear in a counseling client. Each cue must describe one lived-experience signal in 12–30 words. Do not mention PHQ, questionnaires, scores, symptom names, response options, or measurement language. Do not write first-person questionnaire answers or direct paraphrases of the PHQ symptom. Avoid cues that primarily express another PHQ-8 symptom.

Counselor-turn generation.  

> You are the counselor. Given the public conversation so far, the session focus, a brief carryover note, relevant past-session memories, the target counseling move for this turn, and the RealCBT-derived turn form, write only the next counselor utterance in English. Follow the specified surface form, continue the session naturally, avoid repetition and premature closure, and do not mention latent states, PHQ, scores, or hidden generation instructions. Return only the visible counselor message in the required structured form.

Client-turn generation. 

> You are the client described by the private profile. Given the public conversation so far, the current session disclosure beat, private symptom cues, stable background, carryover note, relevant memories, and the RealCBT-derived turn form, write only the next client utterance in English. Express the private cues indirectly through ordinary speech, partial disclosure, hesitation, affect, or concrete examples. Do not name PHQ, scores, questionnaire symptoms, cue lists, or hidden instructions; do not speak like a counselor. Return the visible client message and a short emotion phrase in the required structured form.

Self-report generation. 

> After the session, answer PHQ-8 as the same client using the private symptom cues, stable profile, carryover note, and completed transcript. For each shuffled symptom, choose exactly one of the provided shuffled option texts. Base the answer on what the client would endorse, not on what was explicitly disclosed. Return only the required structured response, with no rationale, commentary, paraphrased options, or extra text.

From symptom scores to dialogue. To express symptom-level scores in dialogue without leaking labels, each PHQ-8 symptom score is translated into private client behaviors rather than visible symptom names or numeric values. The behavior-cue prompt generates short, atomic, naturalistic cues for each PHQ-8 symptom–score cell. It emphasizes lived experience over questionnaire wording and excludes PHQ names, option labels, numeric scores, direct symptom paraphrases, first-person self-report phrasing, and strong cross-symptom contamination. These cues cover affect, functioning, cognition, somatic burden, and interaction style. Candidate cues are first checked within their target cell and then pruned under joint composition tests that sample full PHQ-8 profiles and retain cues whose induced self-reports recover the intended symptom and total scores. After pruning, 16 each PHQ-8 symptom–score cell contains ten validated behavioral cues. At generation time, each symptom contributes five cues; fractional scores are represented by sampling from adjacent integer severity levels in proportion to the fractional value. The client simulator receives these cues as private self-beliefs and is explicitly instructed not to reveal questionnaire language or numeric labels. The conversation generator conditions on the private symptom cues, the PsychEval-derived client profile, memory from earlier visits, and turn-level constraints calibrated from RealCBT [ 34 ]. Coun-selor prompts receive only public dialogue context, a short carryover note, retrieved memories, the session focus, the current counseling-move target, and a RealCBT-derived turn-form card. Client prompts receive the public context plus private symptom cues, stable background, session beat guidance, carryover, retrieved memories, and the same style of turn-form card. RealCBT provides surface calibration rather than clinical supervision: speaker- and stage-specific statistics determine sentence-count patterns, utterance length, question form, discourse starters, and lexical overlap, while the client profile and symptom cues determine the clinical content. This design prevents generated sessions from becoming symptom checklists and instead embeds PHQ-related evidence into realistic counseling exchanges. Visible counselor and client turns are generated with Qwen3.5-35B-A3B under moderate sampling, using temperature 0.6, nucleus mass 0.95 , top-k sampling at 20 , and a 512-token cap per turn. Longitudinal run assignment uses seed 42 , and the inference backend uses a fixed server seed. Each run contains five sessions with 20 counselor turns and 20 client turns per session. Generated turns must be English, role-consistent, non-empty, non-repetitive, free of questionnaire or score leakage, and not prematurely closing; violations trigger targeted repair attempts. 

Latent target and self-report supervision. The target of depression tracking is the underlying latent symptom state, which PHQ questionnaires can only approximate through self-report. In LONG COUNSEL -8, this latent PHQ-8 state is known by construction and therefore serves as the controlled evaluation target. Ideally, models would be trained and tested on this latent state. Real depression datasets, however, cannot observe a person’s true latent depression level and instead provide questionnaire-derived labels. We therefore adopt a realistic and stricter setting: models are trained on simulated self-report PHQ-8 scores but evaluated against the latent PHQ-8 state that drives dialogue generation. The latent values are used only as private generation conditions and never appear in the visible dialogue, so models must infer symptom severity from conversational evidence. After each completed session, the client simulator privately completes PHQ-8 using the same behavior cues, stable profile, carryover note, and transcript. Symptom and option order are deterministically shuffled for each pass, and the prompt requires exact option texts without rationale or extra prose. We generate five deterministic self-report passes and average them symptomwise. Invalid structure, missing symptoms, or paraphrased options trigger a repair prompt; sessions without a valid self-report are excluded from split construction. Session summaries and self-report responses use deterministic decoding, with 1024 tokens allowed for summaries and 1024–1536 tokens for self-report responses depending on the generation setting. 

Dataset statistics and use. The complete corpus contains 3,690 five-session client trajectories and 18,450 labeled counseling sessions. A trajectory-level audit assigns generated clients to fluctuating, stable, improving, and worsening patterns, with all four classes represented. All model experiments use trajectory-level partitions rather than individual-session splits, preventing sessions from the same client from appearing in more than one partition. Filtering is applied before split construction: trajectory reconstruction keeps strict five-visit PSYCHE-D participants, NHANES decomposition uses only complete valid symptom-response rows, the cue bank is pruned to a uniform ten cues per symptom–score cell, and generated runs must contain a valid transcript, latent target, and simulated self-report. The released split manifest is generated over the resulting 3,599 usable trajectories: 2,519 train, 360 validation, and 720 test. Split assignment is produced by sorting run ids, applying a seed-42 random shuffle within the L ONG COUNSEL -8 parent group, and assigning the 0.7/0.1/0.2 train/validation/test ratios at the trajectory level. The manifest stores each run id with its split, so the exact partition can be recovered without relying on filename order. LONG COUNSEL -8 is intended as a controlled evaluation resource, not a substitute for clinical validation. Its role is to expose longitudinal PHQ-8 tracking behavior under known symptom 17 trajectories, partial disclosure, and cross-session continuity. We therefore pair experiments on LONG COUNSEL -8 with evaluation on D AIC -W OZ [ 7], which provides a real single-session anchor for text-based depression assessment.

## F Limitations 

Real longitudinal clinical validation. Although L ONG COUNSEL -8 enables controlled evaluation of longitudinal PHQ-8 tracking and D AIC -W OZ provides a real-data anchor, real multi-session counseling datasets with standardized session-level PHQ supervision remain limited. Therefore, our results should be interpreted as benchmark evidence for robust transcript-based depression tracking, rather than as clinical validation. We plan to extend the evaluation to real longitudinal patient data in collaboration with clinical partners. 23 Open-source upstream models. EmoTrack uses open-source upstream models for utterance embedding and clinical-feature extraction. This design improves accessibility and reproducibility, but may not reflect the strongest performance attainable with frontier closed-source models. Future work can evaluate whether more capable proprietary models improve clinical-feature quality, calibration, and downstream PHQ-8 prediction. 

PHQ-8 prediction versus clinical decision support. Our evaluation focuses on PHQ-8 score prediction. PHQ-8 is a widely used and standardized depression-severity measure, making it an ap-propriate target for controlled comparison; however, predicting PHQ-8 scores does not by itself cover all requirements of clinical decision support. Practical deployment would also require calibration, uncertainty estimation, threshold-based triage analysis, false-negative analysis, privacy safeguards, and human-in-the-loop review. EmoTrack should therefore be viewed as a depression-tracking component rather than an autonomous diagnostic or intervention system.
